## Abstract

Large language models used as judges for ordinal scoring tasks face a tension that no single mitigation resolves: the rater must emit a discrete integer on a small scale, but its training is generative — the rating is a token, not a calibrated probability. This survey reviews the 2023–2026 literature on five coupled questions that together determine whether an LLM-as-judge produces decision-quality numbers: (i) what biases dominate ordinal LLM judgment, (ii) which agreement metrics actually measure calibration as opposed to consistency or monotonicity, (iii) how rubric structure — anchor density, rationale order, demonstration ordering, and output schema — shapes the score distribution, (iv) how confidence and abstention should be elicited so that "insufficient information" does not collapse to the scale's midpoint, and (v) when pointwise, pairwise, or hybrid protocols are the rational design choice. Across approximately 53 primary sources, we find: position bias, scoring-rubric presentation effects, and central-tendency clustering are the empirically dominant failure modes; Pearson r alone systematically masks the additive bias that LLM judges exhibit, requiring ICC(2,1), quadratic-weighted κ, and signed-error metrics in tandem; full per-point anchoring, rationale-before-score, and a free-form rationale block followed by a small structured envelope outperform fully constrained JSON; explicit abstention codes paired with sampled self-consistency aggregation are the only literature-supported remedy for the default-to-midpoint failure mode; and pairwise calibration sampled on the boundary regime where pointwise is least reliable is the dominant hybrid design. We translate these findings into thirteen falsifiable hypotheses (H4A1–A4, H4B1–B3, H4C1–C3, H4D1–D3) that the recalibration milestone's downstream phases can test.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The problem

Ordinal scoring with an LLM judge is harder than either pure classification or pure regression. The model must emit a discrete integer on a short scale (typically 1–5), but its underlying machinery is a generative next-token distribution; the integer it emits is the argmax of a softmax over rating tokens, and the same machinery that emits "3" emits "the" or "of" elsewhere. This creates four structural problems that recur across the literature: (a) the discrete integer collapses the model's internal continuous quality estimate, losing fine-grained signal [10], [20]; (b) when the model lacks evidence, the next-token distribution concentrates on whatever rating token the prompt's prior favors — which, on small ordinal scales, is overwhelmingly the midpoint [44]; (c) the same prompt with different demonstrations, position, or schema can produce systematically different scores even on identical inputs [4], [8], [22]; and (d) inter-rater metrics inherited from psychometrics (Pearson r, ICC(3,1)) are insensitive to additive bias and therefore can declare a high-shifting judge calibrated when a downstream threshold-based decision rule is in fact broken [18].

### 1.2 The motivating system

The four root causes diagnosed in the project that motivates this survey (RC1–RC4 in the spec) instantiate exactly these structural problems. RC1 — candidate profile data never reaches the scorer — exemplifies (c): an upstream pipeline change shifted what the scorer sees without shifting the scorer's prompt. RC2 — profile fragmentation across `config.yaml` and `experience_profile.json` with neither being read at scoring time — is a degenerate case of (a): the model is given an empty signal and must still emit an integer. RC3 — the rubric defaults to "3" and an "all axes ≥ 3 → apply" decision rule produces a 55% apply rate across 5,010 jobs — is the textbook central-tendency failure mode (b). RC4 — Haiku and Sonnet enrichment tiers synthesize JDs from fragments rather than fetching real ones — is again an instance of (a): the discrete integer absorbs the signal-loss of synthesis, and the score becomes uninterpretable.

### 1.3 Research questions

This survey is organized around five research questions, one per topical body section:

- **RQ1 (biases):** Which biases dominate ordinal LLM-as-judge tasks, and what are their measured effect sizes?
- **RQ2 (metrics):** What agreement and calibration metrics correctly diagnose ordinal-judge failure modes that Pearson r and ICC(3,1) miss?
- **RQ3 (rubric):** Which rubric design choices — anchor density, rationale order, few-shot ordering, output schema — are empirically associated with higher human-LLM alignment?
- **RQ4 (confidence):** How should a rubric be designed so that "insufficient information" produces an interpretable abstention rather than collapsing to the scale midpoint?
- **RQ5 (protocol):** When does pointwise scoring suffice, when is pairwise or hybrid worth the cost, and when can a Bradley-Terry calibration anchor recover most of the pairwise quality at fractional cost?

### 1.4 Contributions

We synthesize approximately 53 primary sources across these five questions, identify the regimes in which each technique is the rational choice, and translate the findings into thirteen falsifiable hypotheses for the implementation milestone's variant-A/B comparison. We also flag two areas where the literature is thin relative to our task: central-tendency bias as a *distribution-shape* problem on small ordinal scales (separate from human-survey-style central tendency), and intra-rater non-determinism for non-API-grade local models like qwen2.5:14b, which are increasingly used in cost-sensitive pipelines but understudied in the LLM-judge canon.

## 2. Background

### 2.1 Ordinal scoring fundamentals

Likert-style ordinal scales have three properties that distinguish them from interval scales and that drive most of the methodological complications in this survey. First, the categories are *ordered* but the *intervals* between them are not guaranteed equal — the gap between "1" and "2" need not equal the gap between "4" and "5", and any analysis that treats them as equal (e.g., taking arithmetic means or fitting Pearson r) imposes an unwarranted assumption. Second, the categories are *bounded*, so a rater whose true latent estimate is below 1 or above the maximum must compress; this manifests as floor and ceiling effects. Third, on short scales (e.g., 1–5) the *number of distinguishable judgments* is small, so ties are common and the rater is forced to discard sub-category information. The classical psychometric literature [13], [15], [17] developed agreement coefficients precisely to handle these properties; modern LLM-judge work has had to rediscover much of this terminology, sometimes inventing parallel vocabulary.

### 2.2 LLM-as-judge basics

The LLM-as-judge paradigm, as framed by Zheng et al. [1], reuses a generative language model to produce evaluations of other models' outputs. Two operational modes dominate: pairwise (the judge sees two outputs and picks a winner) and pointwise (the judge sees one output and emits an absolute score). The paradigm's appeal is scaling — strong LLMs achieve over 80% agreement with human pairwise preferences [1] — but the appeal comes with structural caveats. The judge is the same generative architecture as the system being judged; it inherits the same biases and may be more vulnerable in adversarial settings [11]. Its rating is a token-level argmax, which loses information present in the underlying probability distribution [10], [20]. And the rating prompt is itself the measurement instrument: small variations in anchors, demonstration ordering, or schema can shift scores without any change in true quality [8], [28], [30].

### 2.3 The candidate-fit task

The motivating task — scoring how well a candidate's profile matches a job description — is a multi-axis ordinal rating with downstream classification. Each axis (e.g., skills match, experience level, role/level fit, location/timing) is rated 1–5, and a decision rule (currently "all axes ≥ 3 → apply") consumes the joint axis vector to emit a binary recommendation. This means the task has the standard ordinal-judge complications and inherits one further: the joint behavior across axes determines the decision, so per-axis bias compounds. A judge that systematically inflates each axis by 0.5 will inflate the apply rate by far more than 0.5 because the conjunction of "≥ 3 on all axes" is steeper than any single threshold.

## 3. LLM-as-Judge: Core Findings and Bias Taxonomy (RQ1)

### 3.1 Foundations of the paradigm

The LLM-as-judge paradigm was established as a serious alternative to human evaluation by Zheng et al. with their MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena work [1]. Confronting the inadequacy of static benchmarks for measuring open-ended chat-assistant quality, the authors introduced MT-Bench (a curated multi-turn question set) and Chatbot Arena (a pairwise human-preference platform), then asked whether strong LLMs could substitute for the expensive human judges those benchmarks rely on. Across roughly 3,000 expert votes and 30,000 crowdsourced conversations, GPT-4 achieved over 80% agreement with human pairwise preferences — comparable to inter-human agreement on the same items. That headline result legitimized LLM judges as a scalable evaluation tool, but the same paper also enumerated four systematic failure modes: position bias, verbosity bias, self-enhancement bias, and limited reasoning ability. The order in which Zheng et al. listed those biases is itself a useful map of the field's first-order concerns, and most subsequent work either deepens one of those four categories or proposes a structured mitigation for it.

In parallel, Liu et al.'s G-Eval [2] approached evaluation from a different angle — single-output scoring of natural-language-generation tasks rather than pairwise chat comparison. G-Eval combines chain-of-thought reasoning with a form-filling protocol in which the LLM produces sub-scores against rubric dimensions, and reports a Spearman correlation of 0.514 with human ratings on summarization, the strongest result on that benchmark family at publication. Critically, Liu et al. were among the first to surface the *self-bias* phenomenon at scale: GPT-4-as-judge systematically rated GPT-generated summaries higher than equivalently-rated human-written summaries, a finding the authors flagged as a potential issue rather than fully diagnosing. G-Eval's rubric-decomposition protocol also foreshadowed a later debate over pointwise versus pairwise scoring discussed in §7.

The PandaLM project by Wang et al. [3] complemented these closed-source efforts by training an open-source 7B-parameter judge fine-tuned from LLaMA, achieving 93.75% of GPT-3.5's evaluation quality and 88.28% of GPT-4's measured by F1 on a human-annotated test set. PandaLM's contribution to the bias literature is methodological: by treating judgment as a discriminative classification task with subjective criteria (conciseness, clarity, instruction-following, comprehensiveness, formality), it produced training data that downstream bias studies could leverage as a non-API baseline.

### 3.2 The bias taxonomy: position, verbosity, self-preference

The most consequential follow-up to Zheng et al.'s position-bias claim is Wang et al.'s "Large Language Models are not Fair Evaluators" [4]. They demonstrated that swapping the order of two candidate responses can completely flip a pairwise judgment in striking cases — Vicuna-13B was rated superior to ChatGPT on 66 of 80 queries when placed first, but the result reversed when placed second. This is a far more severe operationalization of position bias than [1]'s framing, because it shows the bias is not merely a slight tilt but can dominate the actual quality signal. Wang et al. proposed three mitigations: Multiple Evidence Calibration (require the judge to articulate evidence before scoring), Balanced Position Calibration (run both orderings and aggregate), and Human-in-the-Loop Calibration triggered by a balanced-position diversity entropy threshold. Balanced Position Calibration in particular has become a near-default convention in the field, though it doubles inference cost.

Shi et al. extended this analysis with a systematic study across 15 LLM judges and over 150,000 evaluation instances [5]. Their key contribution is a tripartite metric framework — repetition stability, position consistency, and preference fairness — that lets practitioners decompose what kind of position bias a particular judge exhibits. They find that position bias is "not due to random chance and varies significantly across judges and tasks," that its strength is only weakly correlated with prompt length, and (most usefully for rubric design) that it is *strongly* affected by the quality gap between solutions: when two candidates are close in quality, position effects dominate; when one is clearly better, position bias collapses. This finding has direct implications for ordinal-scoring rubrics that operate near decision boundaries — exactly the regime in which a job-fit pipeline's "borderline 3" scores live.

The self-preference branch was put on rigorous causal footing by Panickssery et al. [6]. They show that GPT-4 and Llama-2 can distinguish their own generations from other models' outputs with non-trivial accuracy, and — by fine-tuning models to vary their self-recognition capability — establish a *linear* causal relationship between self-recognition strength and the magnitude of self-preference inflation. Earlier work [1], [2] had observed self-enhancement; [6] is the paper that demonstrates the mechanism is not a mere correlation but a recognition-driven preference. Wataoka et al. [7] subsequently refined this hypothesis: the apparent self-preference is better explained by *low perplexity* on familiar text. LLMs assign higher scores to lower-perplexity outputs regardless of who generated them, so what looks like narcissism is partly a fluency-calibration problem. This refinement matters for rubric design — anchoring scores to perplexity-stable rubric language (rather than free-form judge prose) should reduce the bias.

### 3.3 Scoring-specific biases: beyond pairwise

A weakness of [1], [4], [5] is that their bias evidence is overwhelmingly pairwise; many production pipelines (including ordinal job-fit scorers) use *pointwise absolute scoring* on a 1–5 or 1–10 scale, where different failure modes apply. Li et al. [8] performed the first dedicated audit of scoring bias in this regime and identified three previously undocumented bias types: rubric order bias (the order in which rubric criteria are presented affects the score), score ID bias (the symbolic identifiers on the scale — "1 to 5" versus "A to E" versus "low/medium/high" — affect the result independently of semantic content), and reference-answer score bias (the score assigned to a provided reference example anchors the judgment of subsequent items). Their experiments on advanced LLMs show all three biases are substantial, though the paper does not publish standardized effect sizes in its abstract. For a recalibration effort that swaps rubric structure, [8] is essential reading: its findings imply that even minor rubric revisions can shift the score distribution without any change in underlying judge competence.

Tripathi et al. [9] complement [8] by directly comparing pairwise versus pointwise feedback protocols under adversarial perturbation. They report that pairwise preferences flip in approximately 35% of cases when distractor features are injected, while absolute (pointwise) scores flip only in 9% of cases. This is a striking inversion of conventional wisdom, which often holds pairwise as more reliable. Tripathi et al. argue absolute scoring is "more robust to manipulation, producing judgments that better reflect response quality." For an ordinal job-scoring rubric this is reassuring — but it sharpens the importance of [8]'s scoring-specific biases, since absolute scoring's robustness only holds if the rubric itself is well-designed.

### 3.4 Central tendency and aggregation

The central-tendency-bias problem — judges clustering scores around the middle of an ordinal scale — has received less direct empirical study than position or verbosity bias, but Wang, Zhang, and Choi [10] supply the most useful methodological intervention for it. Rather than taking the *mode* of a judge's output (greedy decoding's single token), they propose taking the *mean* of the full judgment-token probability distribution, treating the score as expected value over the distribution. Their EMNLP 2025 Findings paper shows this consistently outperforms greedy decoding across evaluation formats, and that incorporating *risk aversion* into the aggregation further improves performance. They also caution that chain-of-thought prompting can compress the distribution and *hurt* performance under their protocol — a direct counterpoint to G-Eval's CoT-driven design [2]. The implication for rubric design is concrete: if the judge's output token distribution is read directly (not just the argmax), central-tendency clustering at the score level can be partially undone post-hoc, because the distribution typically retains discriminative shape even when the mode is "3."

### 3.5 Cross-cutting surveys and cognitive biases

Chen et al. [11] expanded the bias frame beyond purely structural concerns (position, length) to *cognitive* biases borrowed from the psychometric literature: Misinformation Oversight, Gender, Authority, and Beauty biases. They show that both human and LLM judges are vulnerable to perturbations that exploit these biases, and that LLM judges can in fact be *attacked* by crafting inputs that invoke them. This finding undercuts the comfortable narrative from [1] that LLM judges merely mirror human judgment quality — they replicate human cognitive failure modes too, and may be more exploitable in adversarial settings. For an evaluation pipeline that must be robust over time, [11] argues for explicit perturbation testing as part of any rubric-design loop.

Finally, Chan et al.'s ChatEval [12] proposes multi-agent debate as a structural mitigation for the entire bias family rather than addressing biases individually. Their referee-team architecture, in which multiple LLM agents argue across rounds before issuing a verdict, improved evaluation accuracy by 6.2% over single-agent ChatGPT and 2.5% over single-agent GPT-4 on their benchmark. The smaller GPT-4 gain is itself informative: stronger single judges leave less room for ensemble methods, suggesting that for a sufficiently capable scorer, simpler bias-mitigation strategies (balanced position calibration, mean-of-distribution aggregation) may be more cost-effective than multi-agent ensembles.

### 3.6 Synthesis and open questions

Across these papers, several patterns stand out for a recalibration effort. First, *mitigations have measurable but bounded effect sizes*: balanced-position calibration roughly halves the swap-flip rate [4] but does not eliminate it; multi-agent debate yields single-digit accuracy gains [12]; mean-aggregation of judgment distributions improves over greedy decoding consistently but modestly [10]. There is no silver-bullet intervention. Second, *pointwise scoring is structurally distinct from pairwise* and inherits a different bias profile — robust to adversarial pairwise tricks [9] but vulnerable to rubric-presentation effects [8]. Third, the literature on *central tendency bias as a distribution-shape problem* is comparatively thin, especially for ordinal scales of size five or seven; [10] is the most directly relevant intervention but operates on token distributions, not on score outputs. This is a clear gap that the present project may need to address with its own measurement, rather than relying on prior effect-size estimates.

## 4. Ordinal Scoring Methodology and Agreement Metrics (RQ2)

This section reviews the statistical instruments available for evaluating an LLM-as-judge that emits an ordinal score. It surveys the canonical reliability literature — Shrout and Fleiss's intraclass correlation typology, Cohen's weighted kappa, and Krippendorff's alpha — and connects it to recent (2023–2026) findings on how LLM judges actually behave when asked to produce numeric ratings, including the empirical observation that LLM scoring on a continuous 0–100 scale collapses to a small set of preferred values. The aim is to identify which metrics are appropriate for an ordinal-scoring eval harness and, equally important, which metrics are misleading when used in isolation.

### 4.1 The ICC family

The intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) is the standard measure of rater reliability for interval and ordinal data, and Shrout and Fleiss [13] remain the canonical reference for its taxonomy. Their *Psychological Bulletin* paper of 1979 distinguishes six ICC forms generated by crossing three statistical models with two units of analysis. Model 1 (one-way random effects) treats each target as rated by a different randomly drawn judge — appropriate when judges cannot be paired across targets. Model 2 (two-way random effects, absolute agreement) treats both judges and targets as random and is the right choice when generalizing to a wider population of raters and demanding that scores match in absolute value. Model 3 (two-way mixed effects, consistency) treats judges as fixed and measures only whether their relative orderings agree, without penalizing systematic bias. Each model further admits a single-rater (ICC(*model*,1)) or *k*-rater-mean (ICC(*model*,*k*)) variant. The three single-rater forms — ICC(1,1), ICC(2,1), ICC(3,1) — are the relevant ones when a single LLM judge is being audited against a single human ground-truth annotator per item, which is the modal eval-harness configuration. Crucially, ICC(2,1) and ICC(3,1) differ in exactly the way that matters for LLM calibration auditing: ICC(3,1) (consistency) treats a judge that is uniformly two points high as perfectly reliable, whereas ICC(2,1) (absolute agreement) penalizes that systematic shift. A pipeline that has historically over-applied to jobs because the judge skews high will look fine under ICC(3,1) and under Pearson *r*, and broken only under ICC(2,1) or under signed-error metrics.

Shrout and Fleiss's framework remained scattered across applied literatures until Koo and Li [14] consolidated it into an explicit selection guideline. Their 2016 review in the *Journal of Chiropractic Medicine* enumerates ten ICC forms (the original six plus four newer variants), provides a flowchart for matching study design to ICC choice, and proposes the now-widely-adopted interpretive bands of <0.5 (poor), 0.5–0.75 (moderate), 0.75–0.9 (good), and >0.9 (excellent). They emphasize that researchers must report the ICC form explicitly because a study reporting only "ICC = 0.85" is uninterpretable when ICC(3,k) and ICC(2,1) on the same data may differ by 0.2 or more. For an LLM-judge eval harness this argues for two practices: (a) report ICC(2,1) — absolute agreement, single rater — as the primary calibration metric, and (b) treat reliability >0.75 as the bar before downstream decisions are trusted.

### 4.2 Weighted kappa for ordinal disagreement

A complementary tradition descends from Cohen's weighted kappa [15]. The original kappa statistic (Cohen, 1960) treats every disagreement as equally bad — a problem on ordinal scales, where confusing adjacent categories ("3 vs. 4") is qualitatively different from confusing extreme categories ("1 vs. 5"). Cohen's 1968 *Psychological Bulletin* paper extends kappa with a weight matrix that penalizes disagreements proportional to category distance. Two weighting schemes dominate practice: linear weights penalize in proportion to |i − j|, and quadratic weights penalize in proportion to (i − j)². Quadratic weighting is the more widely used choice for ordinal scoring because it is much more lenient about near-misses and steeply intolerant of large errors — which matches human intuition about ordinal mistakes — and because it has the convenient property of equaling a chance-corrected version of the squared-error correlation between rater scores [15]. Quadratic-weighted Cohen's kappa (QWK) became the de facto metric for high-profile ordinal-prediction competitions, including the Hewlett Foundation's Automated Student Assessment Prize and the Kaggle Diabetic Retinopathy challenge, and it is now standard in the ordinal-classification deep-learning literature [16]. The choice between linear and quadratic weighting is not cosmetic: the same prediction-vs-truth confusion table can yield κ_linear ≈ 0.55 and κ_quadratic ≈ 0.78 because the quadratic scheme rewards a judge whose errors are mostly off-by-one. For job-fit scoring on a small ordinal scale (e.g. 1–5), QWK is a defensible primary metric for ordinal-aware agreement, but it does not by itself diagnose whether the *distribution* of judge scores matches the distribution of true scores.

### 4.3 Krippendorff's alpha

Krippendorff's alpha [17] generalizes the agreement-coefficient family further. The 2011 Annenberg School technical report — Krippendorff's most-cited methodological paper — defines α in terms of observed and expected disagreement and admits arbitrary metric difference functions on the categories: nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio, polar, and circular. For ordinal data, the ordinal difference function is δ²(v, v′) = (Σ_{g=v}^{v′} n_g − (n_v + n_v′)/2)², which weights disagreements not just by ordinal distance but by the rank density between the two categories — a category jump that crosses many other ratings is penalized more than a jump across a sparsely populated region. This is conceptually a stricter and more rank-aware penalty than QWK's fixed (i − j)² weighting. Three properties make α particularly attractive over ICC and weighted κ in LLM-judge settings. First, α handles missing data natively, which matters when an enrichment cascade fails on some jobs and the judge produces no score for them. Second, α generalizes to any number of raters without redefinition, so a panel of three LLM judges plus a human gold rater can be summarized by a single coefficient. Third, α is grounded in a unified disagreement-as-distance framework that makes it more directly comparable across nominal (e.g. apply/skip) and ordinal (1–5) outcomes. Krippendorff [17] explicitly recommends α ≥ 0.80 for high-stakes inferences and α ≥ 0.667 as a tentative-conclusion threshold; these are the thresholds that have since been adopted in NLP annotation work [22].

### 4.4 Agreement vs reliability — and why Pearson r misleads

The distinction between *agreement* and *reliability* has been a persistent source of confusion in the applied literature, and Gisev, Bell, and Chen [18] make this explicit in their 2013 review for *Research in Social and Administrative Pharmacy*. Inter-rater agreement (IRA) measures the extent to which raters give *concordant* responses — i.e. their absolute values match — while inter-rater reliability (IRR) measures the extent to which raters *consistently distinguish* between different items. The classic illustrative example: a rater who is always exactly one point lower than the gold rater has IRA = 0 (never identical) but IRR = 1 (perfectly consistent). For an ordinal job-fit scorer this distinction is the entire ball game. A judge that ranks jobs perfectly but inflates every score by +1.5 has perfect IRR (Pearson r = 1.0, ICC(3,1) ≈ 1.0) and disastrous IRA (ICC(2,1) ≈ 0.4, signed mean error = +1.5). Critically, *calibration*, in the sense used in this design document, is closer to IRA than IRR: calibration asks whether the judge's score *matches* the true score on the same scale, not whether it preserves the ranking. Reporting Pearson *r* alone — which captures only monotonic association — therefore systematically understates calibration error in exactly the failure mode that matters for any decision rule that thresholds on absolute score (e.g. "apply if score ≥ 3"). The applied-statistics literature has long noted that Pearson *r* on ordinal data also tends to *underestimate* the strength of monotone but non-linear relationships, so in either direction it is the wrong primary metric for an ordinal-scoring audit [19].

### 4.5 The score-collapse phenomenon

Three recent (2023–2026) LLM-eval papers establish that these classical concerns become acute, not academic, when the rater is an LLM. Zheng et al. [1], in the foundational *Judging LLM-as-a-Judge* paper accompanying MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena, demonstrate that GPT-4-class judges achieve over 80% pairwise agreement with human preferences — comparable to inter-human agreement — but they evaluate primarily on pairwise A/B comparisons, not on absolute ordinal scores. Their main contribution to the present discussion is the cataloguing of judge biases (position, verbosity, self-enhancement) that systematically distort scores rather than introducing pure noise; bias of this form is invisible to Pearson *r* and ICC(3,1) but visible to ICC(2,1), QWK, and signed-error metrics. Liu et al. [2] (G-Eval) propose probability-weighted scoring — taking the LLM's per-score token logprobs and computing an expected score over the rating tokens — explicitly because they observe that direct integer scoring produces an extremely tied score distribution. G-Eval's continuous-expected-score variant achieves Spearman ρ = 0.514 with humans on summarization, substantially above the integer-output baseline; the implication is that the discrete nature of the integer output is itself a source of measurement degradation, even before agreement is computed.

The score-distribution-collapse phenomenon is established directly by Wang et al. [20] in *TrustJudge* (ICLR 2026). They demonstrate explicitly that "score-comparison inconsistency primarily stems from information loss in the integer scoring system — the coarse-grained 5-point scale compresses nuanced quality differences into identical scores," and they show that 23% of ordinal scoring decisions are inconsistent with the same model's pairwise comparisons. Their proposed remedy — distribution-sensitive scoring that computes continuous expectations from the discrete rating-token probabilities — reduces inconsistency by 8.4 absolute percentage points. Although TrustJudge frames the problem on a 5-point scale, the principle generalizes: even when an LLM is prompted on a 0–100 scale, recent work [21] shows the model concentrates probability mass on a small set of preferred multiples (typically values like 0, 25, 50, 75, 80, 85, 90, 95, 100), so the *effective* resolution of a "0–100 scale" is closer to 5–10 distinct values in practice. Li et al. [21] in their 2026 "Grading Scale Impact" study use ICC(2,1) (the Shrout-Fleiss absolute-agreement form) to compare 0–5, 0–10, and 0–100 scales across six benchmarks and find that human-LLM alignment is *highest* on 0–5 and *lowest* on 0–10, with 0–100 in between but inferior to 0–5; they directly attribute the 0–10 and 0–100 deficits to inconsistent rounding and preferred-value clustering. Haldar and Hockenmaier [22] in *Rating Roulette* (EMNLP 2025) extend the picture to *intra-rater* reliability: across multiple runs with different random seeds, the same LLM produces inconsistent scores for the same input, with intra-rater Krippendorff's α frequently below 0.5 — a level the literature [17] regards as unfit for any inferential use. They explicitly recommend Krippendorff's α (rather than Pearson *r*) for diagnosing this failure mode because α is sensitive to absolute disagreement and tolerates the missing-data structure of multi-run experiments.

### 4.6 Methodological consensus

Synthesizing across these sources, the methodological consensus for evaluating an ordinal LLM judge is: (a) Pearson *r* alone is systematically misleading because it is invariant to additive and multiplicative biases that are exactly the failure modes LLM judges exhibit [18], [1]; (b) the agreement metric should be ordinal-aware — quadratic-weighted Cohen's κ [15], [16] or Krippendorff's α with the ordinal difference function [17] — and computed alongside ICC(2,1) for absolute agreement [13], [14]; (c) signed mean error and mean absolute error must be reported because they are the only metrics that surface the systematic bias direction [18]; (d) the eval should separately report calibration (does the judge's score distribution match ground-truth?) from agreement (do individual scores match?) because the LLM-judge literature documents that scores collapse to preferred values even when ranking is preserved [20], [21]; and (e) intra-rater reliability across reseeded runs is itself a metric to track, because the LLM rater is non-deterministic and may produce different scores on the same input [22]. For the recalibration milestone informing this survey, the practical implication is that the eval harness should compute, at minimum: ICC(2,1), quadratic-weighted κ, Krippendorff's ordinal α, mean absolute error, signed mean error, and a histogram of unique values produced — with Pearson *r* retained only as a secondary monotonicity check, not as a primary calibration metric.

## 5. Rubric Design and Scoring Prompt Techniques (RQ3)

When an LLM is asked to assign an ordinal score on a Likert-style scale, the prompt itself is the measurement instrument. Two prompts that elicit the "same" 1–5 score can produce wildly different distributions, inter-rater agreements, and human correlations depending on what anchors are written, whether a reference answer is supplied, when chain-of-thought is emitted, how few-shot examples are ordered, and whether the output is free-form or schema-constrained. This section surveys the rubric and prompt-engineering literature that bears directly on those design choices.

### 5.1 Reference-free scoring with chain-of-thought: G-Eval

The most influential reference-free scoring template is G-Eval [2], which couples a written rubric with a model-generated "chain of evaluation steps" and a form-filling output. Liu et al. show that on SummEval and Topical-Chat, GPT-4 prompted in this way achieves a Spearman correlation of 0.514 with human judges on summarization, substantially above ROUGE and BERTScore baselines. Two design decisions inside G-Eval matter for our purposes. First, the auto-CoT step is generated *once*, ahead of time, and then frozen into the scoring prompt — the rationale is produced before the score, never after. Second, the actual numeric output is constrained to a small integer set and the authors recommend taking a probability-weighted expectation over the score tokens to recover continuous resolution. Liu et al. also flag a worrying limitation: the auto-CoT does not always improve correlation, and on some dimensions it slightly hurts. This is the first piece of empirical evidence that "more reasoning" is not monotonically beneficial — a finding that recurs throughout the literature.

### 5.2 Fine-grained, per-criterion rubrics: FLASK, Prometheus, BiGGen-Bench

Where G-Eval scores a single dimension at a time, a parallel line of work argues that decomposing the rubric into fine-grained skill-specific criteria is itself a calibration intervention. FLASK [23] decomposes evaluation into 12 fine-grained skills (e.g., Logical Correctness, Factuality, Conciseness) grouped under four primary abilities, and assigns a separate 1–5 score per skill with skill-specific anchors. Ye et al. find that this decomposition increases human-LLM agreement and reduces evaluator bias relative to a single coarse "helpfulness" score, and that the gain comes specifically from forcing the evaluator to reason about distinct axes rather than collapsing them into a holistic judgment. The methodological caveat is that FLASK was validated on instruction-following corpora, where skills are reasonably orthogonal; for tasks where criteria are highly correlated (as in candidate-job fit, where "skill match" and "experience level" are entangled), the per-skill gain may be smaller.

Prometheus [24] takes the rubric-decomposition idea further by training a 13B open-source evaluator specifically on user-supplied rubrics. Kim et al. construct the Feedback Collection — 1K fine-grained score rubrics, 20K instructions, 100K GPT-4-generated responses with feedback — and use it to fine-tune Llama-2 13B into Prometheus. The crucial design choice here is that every rubric in the training set is *fully anchored*: each of the five score levels has a written description specific to that instruction's evaluation criterion, not generic "1=poor, 5=excellent" anchors. Prometheus achieves Pearson r = 0.897 with humans across 45 customized rubrics, on par with GPT-4 (r = 0.882) and far above ChatGPT (r = 0.392). The paper also requires a reference answer alongside the rubric, and reports that removing it materially degrades agreement — a finding that directly informs the reference-based vs. reference-free debate.

BiGGen-Bench [25] generalizes the Prometheus approach to 77 tasks and nine capabilities, again with *instance-specific* rubrics anchored at every point of a 5-point Likert scale. Kim et al. evaluate 103 frontier LMs with five evaluator LMs and report that instance-level rubrics close most of the gap between human and LM evaluators that abstract criteria like "helpfulness" leave open. The combined evidence from FLASK, Prometheus, and BiGGen-Bench is that *anchoring every point of the scale* — not just the endpoints — is consistently associated with higher human alignment, and that the labor cost of writing per-instruction rubrics is justified by the calibration gain.

### 5.3 When the rationale should appear

A practical question for any scoring prompt is whether the model should emit its reasoning before, after, or instead of the score. JudgeLM [26] examines this in the fine-tuning regime and identifies three judge-specific biases — position bias, knowledge bias, and format bias — that emerge when models are trained to score with rigid templates. Zhu et al. introduce three mitigations (swap augmentation, reference support, and reference drop), and crucially their training format places the critique before the numeric judgment. They report that this ordering substantially improves agreement with the GPT-4 teacher, exceeding 90% inter-annotator agreement.

Auto-J [27] reaches a similar conclusion from a different angle. Li et al. train a 13B generative judge that emits a structured natural-language critique first and then a final scalar score or pairwise verdict, arguing that the critique-first format is what makes the model's judgments interpretable and post-hoc auditable. While Auto-J does not run the ablation that swaps the order, the consistent design across Prometheus, JudgeLM, and Auto-J — all of which emit reasoning before the score — reflects an emerging consensus that score-first prompting risks the rationale becoming a *post-hoc rationalization* of an already-committed numeric anchor rather than a faithful basis for it. G-Eval's auto-CoT design [2] is the same pattern at the prompt level: reasoning first, score last.

### 5.4 Few-shot calibration and demonstration order

Few-shot examples in scoring prompts function differently than in classification: they teach the model what a "3" or a "4" looks like rather than which class label to emit. Zhao et al.'s influential "Calibrate Before Use" study [28] documents three biases that arise specifically from few-shot demonstrations — *majority label bias* (preferring the most common label in shots), *recency bias* (preferring the label that appears closest to the query), and *common token bias* (preferring tokens frequent in pretraining). The position effect is large enough that simply reordering identical demonstrations can change accuracy by 30+ absolute percentage points on classification tasks. For ordinal scoring, the implication is that few-shot exemplars should be ordered carefully — Zhao et al. recommend either contextual calibration (estimating bias with a content-free probe and subtracting it) or randomization across calibration runs.

The diversity of demonstrations is a second axis. Yang et al. [29] systematically vary demonstration similarity vs. diversity across Llama-3.1, Gemma-2, and Mistral-v0.3 and find that diversity-aware selection improves performance on complex tasks (math, code) and increases robustness to out-of-distribution queries, while similarity-only selection works for simple sentiment-style tasks. For scoring rubrics in domains with edge cases (e.g., a candidate whose experience pattern is unusual), this argues for explicitly including hard-case exemplars rather than only "typical" ones. Diminishing returns past roughly 4–8 shots is a recurring empirical pattern across both [28] and [29], though the exact knee depends on context length and model scale.

### 5.5 Structured output: a calibration trade-off

A common production temptation is to wrap the score in a JSON schema and use constrained decoding. Tam et al. [30] run the most direct test of this assumption to date, comparing free-text, JSON-mode, and constrained-decoding outputs across reasoning, classification, and extraction tasks. They report that strict format constraints cause a measurable decline in reasoning ability — 10–30% on hard reasoning benchmarks — while *helping* on simple classification. The mechanism, they argue, is that the model spends representational capacity on adhering to syntax rather than reasoning. For LLM-as-judge, this trade-off is especially relevant when the rubric demands genuine multi-criterion reasoning: a free-form rationale block followed by a single short JSON envelope at the end may calibrate better than fully nested JSON-from-the-start.

Lee et al. [31] independently study the consistency of LLM evaluators across scales and rubric granularities, and find that strong proprietary models are not necessarily consistent — self-consistency (re-running the same input) and inter-scale consistency (1–5 vs. 1–10) are dimensions on which top models can fail even when their human correlation is high. This bears on rubric design: collapsing to a smaller scale (e.g., 1–4 with no neutral midpoint) and writing dense per-point anchors tends to improve consistency, because the model has less latitude to drift between near-equivalent labels.

### 5.6 Synthesis for rubric design

The literature converges on several actionable choices for an ordinal scoring rubric. First, fully anchor every point of the scale with criterion-specific descriptions ([23], [24], [25]) rather than only endpoints — full anchoring narrows distributional drift and increases human alignment. Second, place the rationale before the score ([2], [26], [27]); score-first prompting risks rationalization rather than reasoning. Third, prefer per-criterion decomposition with separate scores when criteria are reasonably independent [23], reverting to a single overall score otherwise. Fourth, when using few-shot exemplars, include diverse and hard cases [29] and either randomize or calibrate against demonstration order [28]. Fifth, treat structured output as a calibration trade-off [30]: a free-form rationale followed by a small structured envelope tends to outperform fully constrained JSON for reasoning-heavy rubrics. Sixth, evaluate the rubric on consistency, not just correlation [31] — two prompts with identical human-correlation scores can have very different self-consistency properties, and the more consistent one is the safer production choice.

## 6. Confidence, Abstention, and "Missing Information" Handling (RQ4)

When an LLM is used as a judge for an ordinal scoring task, its output is structurally indistinguishable whether the model has examined a complete piece of evidence or has guessed at a near-empty input. This section surveys the literature on three coupled questions that bear directly on this failure mode: (i) how to *elicit* a confidence signal alongside a judgment, (ii) how to *measure* the quality of that signal, and (iii) how to design rubrics so a judge can *abstain* rather than be forced into a default-valued numeric guess. The literature points consistently to one conclusion: forcing a numeric ordinal output when the model lacks signal produces predictably biased scores, and the remediation is some combination of explicit abstention codes, calibrated verbalized confidence, and self-consistency aggregation.

### 6.1 Verbalized confidence: from feasibility to caveat

The earliest demonstration that a model can produce a usable verbalized confidence score is Lin, Hilton, and Evans's *Teaching Models to Express Their Uncertainty in Words* [32]. Their core finding is that a fine-tuned GPT-3 can emit phrases such as "90% confidence" or "high confidence" that map to empirically well-calibrated probabilities, *without* using the model's logits. They introduce the CalibratedMath suite as a test harness and show that the verbalized probability remains moderately calibrated under distribution shift, and that the model is sensitive to its own epistemic uncertainty rather than merely imitating human confidence patterns from the training data. The paper's importance is methodological: it establishes that "verbalized probability" — uncertainty as a generated token — is a real, learnable signal and not just a stylistic artifact.

Tian et al. extended this finding into the era of RLHF-aligned models in *Just Ask for Calibration* [33]. They benchmark ChatGPT, GPT-4, and Claude on TriviaQA, SciQ, and TruthfulQA and report a counterintuitive but now widely-replicated result: for RLHF-tuned models, *verbalized* confidences are typically better-calibrated than the model's internal *conditional probabilities*, often reducing the expected calibration error by a relative 50%. They further show that prompting the model to first generate multiple candidate answers and then assign a confidence to each (a "top-k" verbalized elicitation) sharpens calibration further. The implication for ordinal-judge design is direct: when using an instruction-tuned model, the cleanest confidence channel is to ask for it explicitly in the output schema rather than to rely on extracting it from the API's logprobs — a path which is in any case unavailable on many commercial endpoints.

Kadavath et al. study the same question from the *self-evaluation* angle in *Language Models (Mostly) Know What They Know* [34]. They distinguish two confidence targets: P(True) — the probability the model assigns that a previously-generated answer is correct — and P(IK), the probability that the model "knows" the answer at all. Their key positive finding is that larger models are well-calibrated on multiple-choice and true/false formats when probed in the right format, and that P(True) increases appropriately when supporting evidence is added to the context. The matching negative finding is that P(IK) generalizes poorly to new tasks: a model can be calibrated about its self-knowledge in-distribution while being miscalibrated out-of-distribution. This caveat is structurally important for any system that scores a heterogeneous stream of inputs (e.g., job descriptions whose verbosity, format, and content vary), because the calibration measured on a training-like sample may not transfer.

Xiong et al.'s *Can LLMs Express Their Uncertainty?* [35] consolidates and partly contradicts the optimistic Tian et al. result. Across five LLMs (including GPT-4 and LLaMA 2 Chat) and five datasets, they find that black-box verbalized confidences are *systematically overconfident* — the models imitate human-style certainty language even when wrong. They decompose the elicitation pipeline into three knobs (prompting strategy, sampling method, and aggregation across samples) and show that no single prompt heuristic dominates; the most reliable variance reduction comes from *aggregating across multiple samples*, i.e., self-consistency. The takeaway is that "just ask" is necessary but not sufficient: a single verbalized score from a single sample inherits the model's anchoring biases and should be smoothed by repeated sampling.

A subsequent paper by Yang et al., *Calibrating Verbalized Probabilities for Large Language Models* [36], analyses *why* verbalized probabilities are biased and proposes explicit post-hoc rescaling of the discrete verbalized distribution before calibration. Their work is mostly relevant as confirmation that even well-elicited verbalized confidence has *systematic*, not merely random, bias and that the correction is amenable to standard calibration machinery (temperature scaling and its relatives) once the bias is characterized.

### 6.2 Calibration metrics: ECE, Brier score, and what they hide

Three canonical metrics dominate the calibration literature. The Brier score, due to Brier in the meteorological forecasting community [37], is the mean squared error between predicted probabilities and binary outcomes. It is a strictly proper scoring rule — it is uniquely minimized by reporting the true posterior — and it decomposes into a calibration term and a refinement (resolution) term, which makes it useful for diagnosing *whether* a model is biased versus uninformative. Its limitation is that it scales with the marginal base rate, which makes cross-task comparisons fragile.

The Expected Calibration Error (ECE), as reported in modern deep-learning calibration work, was popularized by Naeini, Cooper, and Hauskrecht in their work on Bayesian Binning into Quantiles [38] and brought to the attention of the deep learning community by Guo et al.'s *On Calibration of Modern Neural Networks* [39]. ECE is defined by binning predictions by confidence and computing a weighted average of the gap between bin accuracy and bin confidence. Guo et al.'s contribution beyond metrology was the empirical demonstration that modern deep networks are systematically *over*-confident — the very behavior later replicated for LLMs — and that a single-parameter temperature-scaling fix recovers calibration cheaply on most classification benchmarks. ECE is intuitive and trivially visualized as a reliability diagram, but it is binning-dependent and insensitive to in-bin distribution shape; it also does not directly measure refinement, only calibration. For LLM-as-judge work the practical recommendation that emerges from [33], [35], and [39] is to report ECE *and* either Brier score or a reliability diagram, since ECE alone can be near zero for a model that always predicts the base rate.

A subtler point flagged by [35] is that for *verbalized* confidence, the support of the distribution is discrete and concentrated on round numbers (50%, 70%, 90%, "high", "medium"), so naive ECE binning aligns the bin edges with the model's preferred output values, masking miscalibration. Calibration analyses on verbalized output should either coarsen the bins or use a strictly proper score like Brier, which does not require binning at all.

### 6.3 Selective prediction, abstention, and the central-tendency failure mode

The right framing for a judge that may "lack signal" is *selective prediction* — classification with a reject option. The deep-learning canonical reference is Geifman and El-Yaniv's *Selective Classification for Deep Neural Networks* [40], which shows how to attach a reject mechanism to any softmax classifier so the system predicts only on inputs above a confidence threshold and abstains on the rest, with formal coverage/risk guarantees. Their framework is silent on *how* the confidence score is obtained; it only insists that one exists. Combined with the verbalized confidence literature above, the obvious LLM analogue is: have the judge emit (score, confidence) and route low-confidence items to a separate path (a re-fetch, human review, or a "low_signal" bucket) rather than counting them as numeric predictions.

The LLM-specific instantiation of selective prediction is the abstention literature. Wen et al.'s TACL survey *Know Your Limits* [41] organizes the field across three axes — the query, the model, and human-value alignment — and consolidates the empirical finding that abstention reduces hallucination but is brittle and rarely emerges from instruction tuning alone. Two concrete abstention training methods stand out. Yang et al.'s *Alignment for Honesty* [42] constructs an honesty dataset by substituting wrong or low-confidence responses with "I don't know" and fine-tunes on it; the resulting models abstain more often without losing accuracy on questions they *can* answer. Zhang et al.'s *R-Tuning* [43], which received an Outstanding Paper Award at NAACL 2024, formalizes this as refusal-aware instruction tuning: the model is trained on a refusal-aware dataset constructed from the *intersection* of training questions with the model's own knowledge, so it learns when to defer rather than confabulate. R-Tuning shows that the resulting "ability to abstain" generalizes as a meta-skill across out-of-domain tasks, suggesting abstention is not just a per-domain nicety but a transferable capability.

Kirichenko et al.'s *AbstentionBench* [44] is an important sobering counterpoint. Across 20 datasets covering unanswerable questions, underspecification, false premises, subjective interpretations, and outdated information, they evaluate 20 frontier LLMs and find that abstention is largely unsolved: model scale has almost no effect, and — most surprisingly for design discussions — *reasoning fine-tuning degrades abstention by ~24% on average*, including on math and science where reasoning models are explicitly trained. A judge built on a reasoning-tuned model is therefore *especially* prone to confabulating an ordinal score from thin context.

This is the literature's most direct treatment of the central-tendency / default-to-3 failure mode. When forced into a small ordinal scale (say, 1–5) without an explicit abstention code, an LLM judge confronted with insufficient evidence will, like a human respondent on a Likert survey, gravitate to the midpoint — the established *central tendency bias* of survey methodology. The Likert-scale literature characterizes the midpoint as both a "no-opinion" proxy and a risk-minimizing default under uncertainty. The empirical AbstentionBench result extends this finding to LLM judges: in the absence of a structurally accessible "I cannot judge" output, the model emits a numerically central — and therefore *application-decisive* but epistemically empty — score. The remediation is rubric-level: extend the output schema with an explicit `INSUFFICIENT_INFORMATION` or `low_signal` code so the model has somewhere to put genuine uncertainty other than the numeric midpoint.

### 6.4 Self-consistency as a free confidence signal

Independently of verbalized elicitation, Wang et al.'s *Self-Consistency Improves Chain of Thought Reasoning in Language Models* [45] established that sampling multiple reasoning paths and majority-voting over the final answers improves accuracy on arithmetic and commonsense benchmarks (gains up to +17.9% on GSM8K). The mechanism is relevant beyond accuracy: the *agreement rate* across samples is itself a self-consistency-derived confidence signal that requires no logprobs and no verbalized confidence prompt. Xiong et al. [35] reports that consistency aggregation across multiple samples is in fact the most reliable single technique for taming verbalized overconfidence, generally outperforming any single prompt design. For an ordinal judge this implies a hybrid design: sample N independent scorings, use the inter-sample variance as an implicit confidence, and route high-variance items to the abstention bucket.

### 6.5 Synthesis and design implications

The literature converges on three points relevant to redesigning a forced-ordinal LLM judge:

1. *Forced numeric output collapses to the midpoint when signal is absent.* The Likert central-tendency bias is the human analogue; AbstentionBench [44] shows the LLM analogue is real, severe, and resistant to scaling.
2. *The cleanest confidence channel for instruction-tuned models is verbalized, sampled, and aggregated.* Verbalized confidence beats logprobs for RLHF models [33], but a single sample is overconfident [35]; self-consistency aggregation [45] is the strongest single fix.
3. *Abstention should be a first-class output, not a downstream filter.* The abstention training literature [41], [42], [43] shows that an explicit `INSUFFICIENT_INFORMATION` slot in the rubric, paired with calibrated verbalized confidence on the numeric axes, makes the abstention pathway structurally available rather than backed into the midpoint.

For ECE-class evaluation specifically, the literature recommends pairing ECE with a strictly proper score (Brier [37] or log-loss) and a reliability diagram — and, when working with discrete verbalized confidences, coarsening bins or moving to Brier to avoid the alignment artifact discussed by [35].

## 7. Pointwise vs Pairwise vs Listwise Scoring (RQ5)

A central design choice for any LLM-as-judge pipeline is the elicitation protocol: does the judge emit an absolute score for each item (pointwise), pick a winner between two items (pairwise), or rank a window of K items at once (listwise)? Each choice carries distinct cost, bias, and calibration properties, and the trade-offs do not collapse onto a single dominance ordering. The literature from 2023 through 2026 has converged on the view that pointwise is cheapest and most parallel but suffers scale-drift and central tendency bias; pairwise is more discriminating but quadratic in cost and acutely sensitive to position bias; and listwise sits in between, with throughput advantages but window-internal positional artifacts. A growing body of work shows that hybrid protocols — a pointwise filter followed by pairwise re-ranking on the top-K, or a pointwise scale anchored post-hoc by a Bradley-Terry fit on sampled pairs — often dominate any single-paradigm baseline. This section synthesizes the primary sources behind those claims and identifies the conditions under which each paradigm is the rational choice.

### 7.1 Pointwise scoring: cheap, parallel, but drifty

The pointwise protocol — give the judge one item and ask for a score on a fixed scale, typically 1–5 or 1–10 — is the operational default of most production LLM-as-judge pipelines and is the framing on which both MT-Bench's "single-answer grading" and AlpacaEval's predecessor protocols were built [1], [46]. Pointwise scoring is O(N) in items judged, embarrassingly parallel, and requires no aggregation step. Its weaknesses are equally well documented. Because the judge has no cross-item anchor, scores tend to bunch in the middle of the scale (central tendency bias) and drift over time as the judge's internal calibration shifts; rubric anchors and few-shot exemplars are commonly added precisely to fight this, and Zheng et al. report that even with rubrics, GPT-4 grading exhibits noticeable verbosity and self-enhancement biases on top of any drift [1].

A counterintuitive recent result from Tripathi et al. tempers the conventional wisdom that pointwise is the weaker protocol [9]. In a controlled study where a generator can embed irrelevant "distractor" features intended to manipulate the judge, they show that pairwise preferences flip in roughly 35% of cases, while absolute pointwise scores flip in only about 9%. The mechanism is intuitive: when the judge sees two outputs side by side, any salient surface feature becomes a discriminator; with a single output, that same feature has no contrast partner to attach to. The implication for pipeline design is that pointwise is more robust under adversarial or distribution-shifted inputs even though it is less stable under benign noise — a distinction that older comparisons between the two protocols mostly elide.

### 7.2 Pairwise comparison: more discriminating, costlier, position-biased

The pairwise framing — show the judge A and B, ask which is better, optionally with ties — became the dominant evaluation protocol for LLM head-to-head benchmarks largely because of the influence of MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena [1]. Zheng et al. demonstrated that GPT-4 acting as a pairwise judge agrees with human annotators about 80% of the time on MT-Bench, matching inter-human agreement, but they also documented the now-canonical pairwise pathologies: position bias, verbosity bias, and self-enhancement bias. Their proposed mitigation — running both A-then-B and B-then-A and only counting verdicts that survive the swap — has been widely adopted, but it doubles cost and discards roughly the fraction of comparisons that are most informative.

Shi et al. [5] provide the most systematic position-bias study to date, evaluating over 150,000 instances across 15 LLM judges and proposing three metrics — repetition stability, position consistency, and preference fairness — that decompose where pairwise judges fail. Their key finding is that position bias is only weakly influenced by prompt length but strongly influenced by the quality gap between the two candidates: when A and B are close in quality, position bias dominates. This is precisely the regime in which pairwise comparisons would be most useful for fine discrimination, so the bias attacks the protocol where it should be strongest. Liu et al. complement this from the calibration side with PAIRS (Pairwise-preference Search), arguing that conventional pointwise calibration of LLM evaluators is structurally insufficient and that reframing evaluation as a pairwise ranking problem is the more reliable route to human-aligned scores [47].

In information retrieval, where pairwise prompting was proposed as a ranking primitive rather than an evaluation tool, Qin et al. showed that Pairwise Ranking Prompting (PRP) with a 20B Flan-UL2 model matched the previous best GPT-4 results on TREC-DL 2019/2020 — a model 50× larger — and beat InstructGPT-175B by more than 10% on average NDCG@10, with comparable margins on BEIR [48]. Their explanation is that pairwise prompts pose a strictly easier reasoning task than pointwise (which demands an absolute scale) or listwise (which demands maintaining a global ordering across many items), so smaller models can succeed where they previously could not. The cost of PRP is, however, formally O(N²): an all-pairs sweep over a candidate set of 100 documents requires roughly 10,000 LLM calls, which is why Qin et al. and successors adopt sliding-window or sorting-based heuristics that recover most of the quality at a fraction of the cost.

### 7.3 Listwise scoring: efficient throughput, window-bound

The listwise alternative — show the judge a window of K candidates and ask for a permutation — was popularized by RankGPT, where Sun et al. demonstrated that GPT-4 in a zero-shot listwise setup matches or exceeds supervised neural rankers on standard IR benchmarks [49]. RankGPT's key engineering contribution is a sliding-window strategy: rank from back to front in overlapping windows of size K, propagating winners forward until the global ranking converges. Listwise is asymptotically O(N/K) in LLM calls — a substantial improvement over pairwise — but pays for the gain in two ways. First, the prompt must hold all K candidates plus the query, which strains context windows for long documents and forces aggressive truncation. Second, position effects within the window do not vanish; they reconcentrate. Several follow-ups, including Shi et al. [5], have shown that listwise judges exhibit position bias of comparable magnitude to pairwise, just expressed as systematic preference for the head or tail of the list rather than as A/B order asymmetry.

Zhuang et al. occupy a middle ground with their Setwise approach, which presents the LLM with K candidates per call but asks only for the single most-relevant pick, then iterates with a sorting algorithm [50]. They report that Setwise dominates pointwise on quality and pairwise on cost, while matching listwise's effectiveness on TREC-DL with substantially fewer tokens per document ranked. The Setwise design is essentially a tournament with parallel multi-way matches, and it makes explicit what the pairwise/listwise comparison left implicit: the relevant axis is not "how many items per prompt" but "how much reasoning the model is asked to express per call." Setwise minimizes the per-call output (one identifier) while maximizing the per-call input, which is the right trade for current LLM pricing.

### 7.4 Hybrid designs and pairwise as a calibration anchor

The most influential hybrid in the LLM-judge literature is LLM-Blender [51], which couples a pairwise PairRanker with a generative fusion module to ensemble the outputs of multiple LLMs. PairRanker was the first to demonstrate that a learned pairwise comparator could exhibit higher correlation with strong-LLM-based rankings than any individual base model on the MixInstruct benchmark, validating the broader thesis that pairwise judgments capture finer-grained quality differences than pointwise ones when the goal is to choose a winner from a candidate pool. The architectural lesson — pointwise to filter, pairwise to discriminate at the top — recurs across modern reranking stacks where a fast embedding retriever supplies top-100 candidates to a pairwise or listwise LLM reranker for the final ordering.

The specific question motivating this survey — whether to fit a Bradley-Terry correction to a pointwise scale using sampled pairs — is treated most directly by recent work on judge-aware aggregation. The "LLM-as-a-jury" line of research, exemplified by reference-free comparative-assessment frameworks, extends the classical Bradley-Terry-Luce model with judge-specific discrimination parameters to jointly estimate latent item quality and judge reliability from pairs alone [52]. Their experiments show that such judge-aware Bradley-Terry variants consistently improve on naive averaging or on globally-calibrated baselines across multiple NLG benchmarks. The implication for a pointwise-first pipeline is concrete: sample N pairs (typically O(items log items) suffices for a reasonable Bradley-Terry fit), elicit pairwise judgments only on those pairs, fit a latent-quality posterior, and use it to recalibrate the pointwise scale post-hoc. The marginal cost is bounded — pairs scale far below all-pairs N² — and the literature suggests it routinely closes a meaningful fraction of the gap to a full pairwise tournament.

PRePair offers a complementary hybrid in the opposite direction: pointwise reasoning embedded inside a pairwise prompt [53]. Su et al. extract per-output rationales using pointwise reasoning, then condition the final pairwise verdict on those rationales rather than on the raw outputs. They report a 24.48% average accuracy improvement across six LLMs on the adversarial LLMBar benchmark and small but consistent gains on MT-Bench, supporting their claim that the protocol-level vulnerability identified by Tripathi et al. [9] can be mitigated by injecting pointwise structure into the pairwise call.

### 7.5 When each paradigm wins

The 2023–2026 literature does not anoint a single winner; instead, it maps paradigm to regime. Pointwise dominates when items must be scored independently (e.g., admission filters, retrieval pre-ranking, any pipeline where N grows but K is bounded), when the inputs may be adversarial (Tripathi et al.'s 9% vs 35% flip-rate result [9]), and when downstream consumers need an absolute number rather than a ranking. Pairwise dominates when the goal is to choose among a small candidate pool, when fine-grained quality differences matter, and when a budget for swap-symmetric voting is available [1], [48]. Listwise (and Setwise [50]) dominate in retrieval and reranking workloads with hundreds to thousands of candidates per query, where pure pairwise is infeasible and pointwise is too coarse. Hybrid designs — pointwise filter plus pairwise re-rank on top-K, or pointwise scoring with a Bradley-Terry calibration anchor fit on sampled pairs — appear to dominate any single-paradigm baseline in the empirical results that report both, at marginal cost overhead in the 10–20% range relative to the cheaper of the two underlying protocols.

For a pipeline that is currently pointwise and considering pairwise calibration: the literature supports the addition strongly, with two qualifications. First, the pairs sampled for the Bradley-Terry fit should be drawn primarily from the regime where pointwise is least reliable (clusters of items with near-equal pointwise scores), because pairwise's information advantage over pointwise is largest exactly there. Second, every pairwise call should be run swap-symmetric and the verdict counted only on agreement, in line with the Zheng et al. protocol [1] and confirmed by Shi et al. [5]; the doubled cost is the price of avoiding position bias, and there is no principled way to do without it.

## 8. Discussion

### 8.1 What the literature settles

Several findings recur across the five topical sections with enough consistency that they should be treated as settled for engineering purposes. First, *position bias and order effects dominate among pairwise pathologies and resist single-shot mitigation*: balanced-position calibration [4], swap-symmetric voting [1], and Setwise tournaments [50] each reduce but do not eliminate position effects, and the reduction comes at a 2× to 3× cost penalty. Second, *Pearson r alone is the wrong primary metric for ordinal judge evaluation* [18]: it is invariant to the additive shift that defines the most consequential LLM-judge failure mode (uniform inflation triggering a cascade of false-positive applies). Third, *fully anchoring every score level — not just endpoints — improves human-LLM alignment* across both fine-tuned [24] and prompted [25] judges. Fourth, *rationale-before-score outperforms score-first* in every paper that ablates the order [2], [26], [27]. Fifth, *forced numeric output collapses to the midpoint when signal is absent*, and the only structurally sound remediation is an explicit abstention code in the output schema [44], paired with sampled self-consistency aggregation [45].

### 8.2 What the literature disputes

The literature is genuinely unsettled on two important questions. First, *whether chain-of-thought helps or hurts ordinal scoring*: G-Eval reports CoT hurts on some dimensions [2], Wang/Zhang/Choi report CoT compresses the judgment-token distribution and degrades mean-aggregated scoring [10], yet Prometheus and JudgeLM report critique-first formats improve agreement [24], [26]. The reconciling hypothesis — that CoT helps when the rubric is well-anchored and hurts when it is loose — is suggestive but not directly tested. Second, *whether pairwise is more reliable than pointwise*. The conventional wisdom inherited from preference-tuning literature [1] holds that pairwise is more reliable; Tripathi et al. [9] invert this in adversarial settings (35% vs 9% flip rate), Liu et al. [47] argue pointwise is structurally insufficient for calibration, and Tam et al. [30] further muddy the water by showing constraint-induced reasoning loss favors freer formats. The honest summary is that the right protocol depends on the input distribution, the cost budget, and whether the downstream consumer needs absolute scores or rankings.

### 8.3 Surprises

Three findings challenge intuition strongly enough to warrant explicit mention. First, *reasoning fine-tuning degrades abstention* by ~24% on average across 20 datasets [44]. The implication is that a "smarter" judge built on a chain-of-thought-tuned base is *more* likely to confabulate from thin context, not less — a direct contradiction of the popular framing that reasoning-tuned models are safer. Second, *0–100 scales effectively collapse to 5–10 unique values*, with peak human-LLM alignment occurring on 0–5 scales rather than larger ones [21]. The "more granularity is always better" intuition fails because the model concentrates probability mass on multiples of 5, 10, and 25 regardless of the prompted scale. Third, *strict format constraints can cost 10–30% on reasoning benchmarks* [30] — counter to the production assumption that JSON-mode is "free" structure. A free-form rationale followed by a small structured envelope outperforms fully-nested JSON for reasoning-heavy rubrics.

### 8.4 Gaps relevant to the motivating system

Three gaps in the literature deserve explicit flagging because they directly affect the recalibration milestone's evaluation design. First, *intra-rater non-determinism for non-API local models* (qwen2.5:14b, llama-3.1:8b, etc.) is understudied: most LLM-judge papers run on GPT-4 / Claude / Gemini, which are deterministic at temperature=0 in ways that local Ollama-served models often are not. Haldar and Hockenmaier's *Rating Roulette* [22] is the most relevant prior work but tests proprietary models. The Phase 33 milestone documented qwen2.5:14b non-determinism explicitly; a recalibration eval must therefore measure intra-rater Krippendorff α across reseeded runs as a first-class metric. Second, *central-tendency bias as a distribution-shape problem on small ordinal scales* (1–5, 1–7) is comparatively thin in the LLM-judge literature relative to its frequency as a production failure mode; the Likert-scale literature is the more mature reference and the LLM-judge field has not yet operationalized its findings. Third, *the pairwise-as-calibration-anchor design with Bradley-Terry post-hoc fit* is described in [52] for comparative assessment but not benchmarked head-to-head against pure pointwise rubric improvements; whether it is worth the additional inference cost on top of better-anchored pointwise rubrics is an open empirical question that this milestone may have to answer with its own data.

### 8.5 Synthesis: a connected argument

Reading across topics, a single connected argument emerges. The *default-to-3 failure mode* is the convergence point of three problems: (a) the discrete integer output discards the model's continuous quality estimate [10], [20], (b) the rubric forces a numeric guess when signal is absent rather than offering an abstention slot [44], and (c) the eval harness uses metrics (Pearson r, ICC(3,1)) that are insensitive to the additive bias this default creates [18]. Fixing one without the others is insufficient: better metrics will diagnose the problem but not fix it; better rubrics with abstention will reduce midpoint clustering but won't recover the lost continuous resolution; and reading the token distribution will recover resolution but won't address the case where the model lacks information entirely. The integrated remediation that the literature converges toward is: (1) eval on ICC(2,1), QWK, ordinal Krippendorff α, signed MAE, and a unique-values histogram, with intra-rater α across reseeds [13], [14], [15], [17], [18]; (2) rubric with full per-point anchors, rationale-before-score, free-form rationale + small JSON envelope, and an explicit `INSUFFICIENT_INFORMATION` code [24], [25], [27], [30], [44]; (3) optional pairwise calibration anchor sampled on the borderline-pointwise regime with swap-symmetric voting [9], [52]. The recalibration milestone's variant-A/B comparison should be parameterized over exactly these levers.

## 9. Research Proposal: From Literature to Falsifiable Hypotheses

This section reframes the implementation milestone (Phases 2–6 in the spec) as a research proposal. Each of thirteen Phase 4 dimensions is restated as a falsifiable hypothesis with literature grounding, expected directional outcome, and the eval harness primitive that would falsify it.

### 9.1 Hypotheses

**Group A — Rubric structure and anchoring:**

- **H4A1.** Stricter axis thresholds (e.g., requiring `≥ 4` rather than `≥ 3` on every axis to apply) reduce the apply-FP rate without harming consider precision. *Grounding:* Li et al.'s scoring-bias study [8] documents threshold sensitivity in scoring rubrics; central tendency clusters at the midpoint [44]; the current 55% apply rate at "all axes ≥ 3" is consistent with midpoint clustering rather than genuine high-quality matches. *Falsifier:* if the gold-set ROC curve places the optimal threshold at 3 rather than 4 even with a recalibrated rubric.

- **H4A2.** Anchoring every score level with criterion-specific descriptions (rather than only endpoints) reduces central-tendency clustering and improves ICC(2,1). *Grounding:* Prometheus [24] and BiGGen-Bench [25] both rely on full per-point anchoring and report Pearson r > 0.85 with humans; FLASK [23] confirms the gain from rubric decomposition. *Falsifier:* unique-values histogram concentration ratio at score=3 does not decrease in variant A vs. baseline.

- **H4A3.** Rationale-before-score outperforms score-only or score-then-justify formats on QWK and signed-error magnitude. *Grounding:* G-Eval [2], JudgeLM [26], Auto-J [27], and the broader CoT-evaluation literature. *Falsifier:* variant A's QWK is not higher than baseline, or variant A's signed-MAE is not closer to zero.

- **H4A4.** A 1–5 scale outperforms 1–7 and 1–10 on ICC(2,1) and unique-values count when the underlying judge is qwen2.5:14b. *Grounding:* Li et al.'s grading-scale study [21] reports peak alignment on 0–5; preferred-value clustering on larger scales [21], [22]. *Falsifier:* a back-to-back run on 1–5 vs 1–10 with identical rubrics shows ≤0.05 ICC(2,1) gap or higher unique-values count on 1–10.

**Group B — Profile injection format:**

- **H4B1.** Structured-block profile injection (skills, experience, location, target roles each in a labeled field) improves per-axis ICC(2,1) over free-text profile injection. *Grounding:* Tam et al.'s structured-output trade-off [30] is bidirectional — for *input* parsing, structure helps; the cost is on *output* reasoning. Profile-as-input parses easier when structured. *Falsifier:* ICC(2,1) on the "skills match" axis does not improve when profile is structured vs. free-text.

- **H4B2.** Single-block consolidated profile injection beats fragmented multi-source injection (e.g., merging `config.yaml.profile` and `experience_profile.json` once at scoring time). *Grounding:* RC2 in the spec; Kadavath et al.'s in-context evidence finding [34] (P(True) increases when supporting evidence is added in a coherent block). *Falsifier:* fragmented vs. consolidated injection produces equivalent ICC(2,1) on a held-out gold set.

- **H4B3.** Including 3–5 keyword anchors specific to the candidate's recent technical stack improves precision on apply-FP without hurting consider recall. *Grounding:* Kim et al. [25] on instance-specific rubrics. *Falsifier:* keyword-anchor variant produces lower precision-at-apply on the gold set.

**Group C — Confidence and abstention design:**

- **H4C1.** Adding an explicit `INSUFFICIENT_INFORMATION` axis-level code reduces midpoint clustering by ≥ 30% on jobs lacking JD enrichment. *Grounding:* Wen et al. [41], Zhang et al. [43], and Kirichenko et al. [44]; the failure mode is established and the remediation is rubric-level. *Falsifier:* unique-values histogram still shows ≥ 50% mass at "3" on the JD-empty subset of the gold set after adding the abstention code.

- **H4C2.** Verbalized confidence on a 0–1 scale (one continuous probability per axis) plus 3-sample self-consistency variance produces a confidence signal that, when used to filter low-confidence scores out of the apply pool, lifts apply precision by ≥ 0.10 at constant recall. *Grounding:* Tian et al. [33] for verbalized-as-confidence, Xiong et al. [35] for sampling necessity, Wang et al. [45] for variance as confidence. *Falsifier:* the confidence-based filter does not move the apply-precision–recall curve.

- **H4C3.** A pairwise calibration anchor — sampling 200 borderline pairs (pointwise scores within 0.5 of each other) and fitting a Bradley-Terry correction — improves QWK on the gold set by ≥ 0.05 absolute, beyond a baseline of better-anchored pointwise alone. *Grounding:* Qian et al. [52] on judge-aware Bradley-Terry; Shi et al. [5] on quality-gap-dependent position bias; the targeted-sampling argument in Section 7.5. *Falsifier:* the BT-corrected scores do not improve QWK over the pointwise variant by ≥ 0.05.

**Group D — Enrichment cascade and downstream propagation:**

- **H4D1.** Real fetched JDs produce scores with strictly lower MAE against the human gold than Haiku- or Sonnet-synthesized JDs from fragments. *Grounding:* RC4 in the spec; Kadavath et al. [34] on evidence-conditioned calibration; G-Eval [2] on input-quality dependence. *Falsifier:* MAE on real-JD-scored items is not lower than MAE on synthesized-JD items at p < 0.05.

- **H4D2.** Enrichment failure must be propagated as a `low_signal` classification, not as a silent score default. *Grounding:* AbstentionBench [44]; the central-tendency literature [11]. This is structural rather than empirical — the falsifier is whether implementing the propagation reduces midpoint clustering on enrichment-failure items by ≥ 50%. *Falsifier:* same as H4C1 but restricted to enrichment-failure items.

- **H4D3.** The marginal calibration improvement from real-JD enrichment justifies its cost relative to a no-enrichment baseline (cost-per-improved-decision metric). *Grounding:* This is a cost-benefit hypothesis grounded in [9]'s finding that input quality matters less for pointwise than for pairwise. *Falsifier:* gold-set score MAE with vs. without enrichment differs by < 0.2 axis units; if so, enrichment is not justified.

### 9.2 Methodology

The Phase 5 (gold set + harness) and Phase 6 (rollout) plans operationalize the falsifiers above. Briefly: a 40-row gold set with two human annotations per row, each scored independently on the 1–5 scale across all axes; a harness that computes ICC(2,1), QWK, ordinal Krippendorff α, signed MAE, MAE, unique-values histogram, and intra-rater α across 5 reseeded runs; and acceptance gates that require ICC(2,1) ≥ 0.7 and apply-rate ∈ [10%, 25%] before rollout. The variant-A/B comparison is a within-subject test on the same gold set, with rubric A and rubric B both scoring all 40 rows; the falsifier rejection is per-hypothesis at α = 0.05 with appropriate multiple-testing correction.

### 9.3 Expected outcomes

Based on the literature, the predicted directional outcomes are:
- H4A1, H4A2, H4A3, H4A4: all confirmed in direction; effect sizes likely small (0.05–0.15 ICC(2,1) gain each), consistent with the field's published bounded-mitigation pattern [4], [10], [12].
- H4B1, H4B2: likely confirmed; H4B3 less certain because keyword anchoring can over-fit.
- H4C1: strongly predicted to be confirmed (the abstention literature is unambiguous on direction).
- H4C2: predicted to be confirmed but with high variance across axes; per [44], the model may not abstain even with the slot.
- H4C3: predicted to be confirmed but with marginal effect (≤ 0.05 QWK), insufficient to justify the inference cost given a well-anchored pointwise baseline already in place.
- H4D1, H4D2, H4D3: predicted to be confirmed strongly; the existing project's RC4 diagnosis is directly aligned with [34], [44].

### 9.4 Validity threats

Four threats deserve explicit attention:

1. **Small-N gold set.** N=40 limits statistical power; a 0.05 QWK difference is below detection threshold at this N. Mitigation: prefer larger effect-size hypotheses for go/no-go decisions, treat smaller effects as preliminary signals.
2. **Single-labeler variance.** Each row has at most two human annotations. ICC(2,1) is undefined for single-rater-per-target; a second-rater overlap on 100% of items is required. Mitigation: enforce dual annotation explicitly in Phase 5.
3. **qwen2.5:14b non-determinism.** Documented in the project's Phase 33 milestone. Confirmed by [22] for proprietary models. Mitigation: 5-seed averaging on every gold-set evaluation; intra-rater Krippendorff α reported as a first-class metric.
4. **Distributional shift.** The 5,010-job production stream is not a random sample of the gold set; over time, JD format, hiring-platform mix, and role distribution will drift. Mitigation: production monitoring of the unique-values histogram and apply-rate as proxies for distributional drift, with re-eval triggered when either crosses a band.

---

## 10. Conclusion

The LLM-as-judge literature for ordinal scoring tasks has matured rapidly between 2023 and 2026 and now offers a coherent set of design recommendations: full per-point anchoring, rationale-before-score, small ordinal scales (1–5), explicit abstention codes, sampled self-consistency for confidence, ordinal-aware metrics (ICC(2,1), QWK, Krippendorff α) reported alongside signed-error magnitude, and pairwise calibration anchors sampled on the borderline regime when cost permits. The motivating job-fit pipeline's four root causes map cleanly onto failure modes the literature anticipates: the signal-collapse from synthesized JDs, the midpoint clustering when profile data is absent, and the misleading historical reliance on Pearson r alone. The implementation milestone's variant-A/B comparison should test exactly these levers, with the thirteen hypotheses above as falsifiable predictions. Where the literature is thin — intra-rater non-determinism on local models, small-scale central-tendency dynamics, and head-to-head benchmarking of pointwise rubric improvements vs. pairwise calibration anchors — the milestone's own data will be a contribution to the field, not just a project deliverable.
