# Topic 5: Pointwise vs Pairwise vs Listwise Scoring

## Introduction

A central design choice for any LLM-as-judge pipeline is the elicitation protocol: does the judge emit an absolute score for each item (pointwise), pick a winner between two items (pairwise), or rank a window of K items at once (listwise)? Each choice carries distinct cost, bias, and calibration properties, and the trade-offs do not collapse onto a single dominance ordering. The literature from 2023 through 2026 has converged on the view that pointwise is cheapest and most parallel but suffers scale-drift and central tendency bias; pairwise is more discriminating but quadratic in cost and acutely sensitive to position bias; and listwise sits in between, with throughput advantages but window-internal positional artifacts. A growing body of work shows that hybrid protocols — a pointwise filter followed by pairwise re-ranking on the top-K, or a pointwise scale anchored post-hoc by a Bradley-Terry fit on sampled pairs — often dominate any single-paradigm baseline. This section synthesizes the primary sources behind those claims and identifies the conditions under which each paradigm is the rational choice.

## Pointwise scoring: cheap, parallel, but drifty

The pointwise protocol — give the judge one item and ask for a score on a fixed scale, typically 1-5 or 1-10 — is the operational default of most production LLM-as-judge pipelines and is the framing on which both MT-Bench's "single-answer grading" and AlpacaEval's predecessor protocols were built [1], [2]. Pointwise scoring is O(N) in items judged, embarrassingly parallel, and requires no aggregation step. Its weaknesses are equally well documented. Because the judge has no cross-item anchor, scores tend to bunch in the middle of the scale (central tendency bias) and drift over time as the judge's internal calibration shifts; rubric anchors and few-shot exemplars are commonly added precisely to fight this, and Zheng et al. report that even with rubrics, GPT-4 grading exhibits noticeable verbosity and self-enhancement biases on top of any drift [1].

A counterintuitive recent result from Tripathi et al. tempers the conventional wisdom that pointwise is the weaker protocol [3]. In a controlled study where a generator can embed irrelevant "distractor" features intended to manipulate the judge, they show that pairwise preferences flip in roughly 35% of cases, while absolute pointwise scores flip in only about 9%. The mechanism is intuitive: when the judge sees two outputs side by side, any salient surface feature becomes a discriminator; with a single output, that same feature has no contrast partner to attach to. The implication for pipeline design is that pointwise is more robust under adversarial or distribution-shifted inputs even though it is less stable under benign noise — a distinction that older comparisons between the two protocols mostly elide.

## Pairwise comparison: more discriminating, costlier, position-biased

The pairwise framing — show the judge A and B, ask which is better, optionally with ties — became the dominant evaluation protocol for LLM head-to-head benchmarks largely because of the influence of MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena [1]. Zheng et al. demonstrated that GPT-4 acting as a pairwise judge agrees with human annotators about 80% of the time on MT-Bench, matching inter-human agreement, but they also documented the now-canonical pairwise pathologies: position bias (a strong preference for the first or last option), verbosity bias (longer answers preferred regardless of quality), and self-enhancement bias (the judge favors outputs from its own model family). Their proposed mitigation — running both A-then-B and B-then-A and only counting verdicts that survive the swap — has been widely adopted, but it doubles cost and discards roughly the fraction of comparisons that are most informative.

Shi et al. provide the most systematic position-bias study to date, evaluating over 150,000 instances across 15 LLM judges and proposing three metrics — repetition stability, position consistency, and preference fairness — that decompose where pairwise judges fail [4]. Their key finding is that position bias is only weakly influenced by prompt length but strongly influenced by the quality gap between the two candidates: when A and B are close in quality, position bias dominates. This is precisely the regime in which pairwise comparisons would be most useful for fine discrimination, so the bias attacks the protocol where it should be strongest. Liu et al. complement this from the calibration side with PAIRS (Pairwise-preference Search), arguing that conventional pointwise calibration of LLM evaluators is structurally insufficient and that reframing evaluation as a pairwise ranking problem is the more reliable route to human-aligned scores [5].

In information retrieval, where pairwise prompting was proposed as a ranking primitive rather than an evaluation tool, Qin et al. showed that Pairwise Ranking Prompting (PRP) with a 20B Flan-UL2 model matched the previous best GPT-4 results on TREC-DL 2019/2020 — a model 50x larger — and beat InstructGPT-175B by more than 10% on average NDCG@10, with comparable margins on BEIR [6]. Their explanation is that pairwise prompts pose a strictly easier reasoning task than pointwise (which demands an absolute scale) or listwise (which demands maintaining a global ordering across many items), so smaller models can succeed where they previously could not. The cost of PRP is, however, formally O(N^2): an all-pairs sweep over a candidate set of 100 documents requires roughly 10,000 LLM calls, which is why Qin et al. and successors adopt sliding-window or sorting-based heuristics that recover most of the quality at a fraction of the cost.

## Listwise scoring: efficient throughput, window-bound

The listwise alternative — show the judge a window of K candidates and ask for a permutation — was popularized by RankGPT, where Sun et al. demonstrated that GPT-4 in a zero-shot listwise setup matches or exceeds supervised neural rankers on standard IR benchmarks [7]. RankGPT's key engineering contribution is a sliding-window strategy: rank from back to front in overlapping windows of size K, propagating winners forward until the global ranking converges. Listwise is asymptotically O(N/K) in LLM calls — a substantial improvement over pairwise — but pays for the gain in two ways. First, the prompt must hold all K candidates plus the query, which strains context windows for long documents and forces aggressive truncation. Second, position effects within the window do not vanish; they reconcentrate. Several follow-ups, including Shi et al. [4], have shown that listwise judges exhibit position bias of comparable magnitude to pairwise, just expressed as systematic preference for the head or tail of the list rather than as A/B order asymmetry.

Zhuang et al. occupy a middle ground with their Setwise approach, which presents the LLM with K candidates per call but asks only for the single most-relevant pick, then iterates with a sorting algorithm [8]. They report that Setwise dominates pointwise on quality and pairwise on cost, while matching listwise's effectiveness on TREC-DL with substantially fewer tokens per document ranked. The Setwise design is essentially a tournament with parallel multi-way matches, and it makes explicit what the pairwise/listwise comparison left implicit: the relevant axis is not "how many items per prompt" but "how much reasoning the model is asked to express per call." Setwise minimizes the per-call output (one identifier) while maximizing the per-call input, which is the right trade for current LLM pricing.

## Hybrid designs and pairwise as a calibration anchor

The most influential hybrid in the LLM-judge literature is LLM-Blender [9], which couples a pairwise PairRanker with a generative fusion module to ensemble the outputs of multiple LLMs. PairRanker was the first to demonstrate that a learned pairwise comparator could exhibit higher correlation with strong-LLM-based rankings than any individual base model on the MixInstruct benchmark, validating the broader thesis that pairwise judgments capture finer-grained quality differences than pointwise ones when the goal is to choose a winner from a candidate pool. The architectural lesson — pointwise to filter, pairwise to discriminate at the top — recurs across modern reranking stacks where a fast embedding retriever supplies top-100 candidates to a pairwise or listwise LLM reranker for the final ordering.

The specific question motivating this survey — whether to fit a Bradley-Terry correction to a pointwise scale using sampled pairs — is treated most directly by recent work on judge-aware aggregation. The "LLM-as-a-jury" line of research, exemplified by reference-free comparative-assessment frameworks, extends the classical Bradley-Terry-Luce model with judge-specific discrimination parameters to jointly estimate latent item quality and judge reliability from pairs alone [10]. Their experiments show that such judge-aware Bradley-Terry variants consistently improve on naive averaging or on globally-calibrated baselines across multiple NLG benchmarks. The implication for a pointwise-first pipeline is concrete: sample N pairs (typically O(items log items) suffices for a reasonable Bradley-Terry fit), elicit pairwise judgments only on those pairs, fit a latent-quality posterior, and use it to recalibrate the pointwise scale post-hoc. The marginal cost is bounded — pairs scale far below all-pairs N^2 — and the literature suggests it routinely closes a meaningful fraction of the gap to a full pairwise tournament.

PRePair offers a complementary hybrid in the opposite direction: pointwise reasoning embedded inside a pairwise prompt [11]. Su et al. extract per-output rationales using pointwise reasoning, then condition the final pairwise verdict on those rationales rather than on the raw outputs. They report a 24.48% average accuracy improvement across six LLMs on the adversarial LLMBar benchmark and small but consistent gains on MT-Bench, supporting their claim that the protocol-level vulnerability identified by Tripathi et al. [3] can be mitigated by injecting pointwise structure into the pairwise call.

## When each paradigm wins

The 2023-2026 literature does not anoint a single winner; instead, it maps paradigm to regime. Pointwise dominates when items must be scored independently (e.g., admission filters, retrieval pre-ranking, any pipeline where N grows but K is bounded), when the inputs may be adversarial (Tripathi et al.'s 9% vs 35% flip-rate result [3]), and when downstream consumers need an absolute number rather than a ranking. Pairwise dominates when the goal is to choose among a small candidate pool, when fine-grained quality differences matter, and when a budget for swap-symmetric voting is available [1], [6]. Listwise (and Setwise [8]) dominate in retrieval and reranking workloads with hundreds to thousands of candidates per query, where pure pairwise is infeasible and pointwise is too coarse. Hybrid designs — pointwise filter plus pairwise re-rank on top-K, or pointwise scoring with a Bradley-Terry calibration anchor fit on sampled pairs — appear to dominate any single-paradigm baseline in the empirical results that report both, at marginal cost overhead in the 10-20% range relative to the cheaper of the two underlying protocols.

For a pipeline that is currently pointwise and considering pairwise calibration: the literature supports the addition strongly, with two qualifications. First, the pairs sampled for the Bradley-Terry fit should be drawn primarily from the regime where pointwise is least reliable (clusters of items with near-equal pointwise scores), because pairwise's information advantage over pointwise is largest exactly there. Second, every pairwise call should be run swap-symmetric and the verdict counted only on agreement, in line with the Zheng et al. protocol [1] and confirmed by Shi et al. [4]; the doubled cost is the price of avoiding position bias, and there is no principled way to do without it.
