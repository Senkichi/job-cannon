# Topic 2: Ordinal Scoring Methodology and Agreement Metrics

## Scope

This section reviews the statistical instruments available for evaluating an LLM-as-judge that emits an ordinal score. It surveys the canonical reliability literature — Shrout and Fleiss's intraclass correlation typology, Cohen's weighted kappa, and Krippendorff's alpha — and connects it to recent (2023–2026) findings on how LLM judges actually behave when asked to produce numeric ratings, including the empirical observation that LLM scoring on a continuous 0–100 scale collapses to a small set of preferred values. The aim is to identify which metrics are appropriate for an ordinal-scoring eval harness and, equally important, which metrics are misleading when used in isolation.

## Body

The intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) is the standard measure of rater reliability for interval and ordinal data, and Shrout and Fleiss [1] remain the canonical reference for its taxonomy. Their *Psychological Bulletin* paper of 1979 distinguishes six ICC forms generated by crossing three statistical models with two units of analysis. Model 1 (one-way random effects) treats each target as rated by a different randomly drawn judge — appropriate when judges cannot be paired across targets. Model 2 (two-way random effects, absolute agreement) treats both judges and targets as random and is the right choice when generalizing to a wider population of raters and demanding that scores match in absolute value. Model 3 (two-way mixed effects, consistency) treats judges as fixed and measures only whether their relative orderings agree, without penalizing systematic bias. Each model further admits a single-rater (ICC(*model*,1)) or *k*-rater-mean (ICC(*model*,*k*)) variant. The three single-rater forms — ICC(1,1), ICC(2,1), ICC(3,1) — are the relevant ones when a single LLM judge is being audited against a single human ground-truth annotator per item, which is the modal eval-harness configuration. Crucially, ICC(2,1) and ICC(3,1) differ in exactly the way that matters for LLM calibration auditing: ICC(3,1) (consistency) treats a judge that is uniformly two points high as perfectly reliable, whereas ICC(2,1) (absolute agreement) penalizes that systematic shift. A pipeline that has historically over-applied to jobs because the judge skews high will look fine under ICC(3,1) and under Pearson *r*, and broken only under ICC(2,1) or under signed-error metrics.

Shrout and Fleiss's framework remained scattered across applied literatures until Koo and Li [2] consolidated it into an explicit selection guideline. Their 2016 review in the *Journal of Chiropractic Medicine* enumerates ten ICC forms (the original six plus four newer variants), provides a flowchart for matching study design to ICC choice, and proposes the now-widely-adopted interpretive bands of <0.5 (poor), 0.5–0.75 (moderate), 0.75–0.9 (good), and >0.9 (excellent). They emphasize that researchers must report the ICC form explicitly because a study reporting only "ICC = 0.85" is uninterpretable when ICC(3,k) and ICC(2,1) on the same data may differ by 0.2 or more. For an LLM-judge eval harness this argues for two practices: (a) report ICC(2,1) — absolute agreement, single rater — as the primary calibration metric, and (b) treat reliability >0.75 as the bar before downstream decisions are trusted.

A complementary tradition descends from Cohen's weighted kappa [3]. The original kappa statistic (Cohen, 1960) treats every disagreement as equally bad — a problem on ordinal scales, where confusing adjacent categories ("3 vs. 4") is qualitatively different from confusing extreme categories ("1 vs. 5"). Cohen's 1968 *Psychological Bulletin* paper extends kappa with a weight matrix that penalizes disagreements proportional to category distance. Two weighting schemes dominate practice: linear weights penalize in proportion to |i − j|, and quadratic weights penalize in proportion to (i − j)². Quadratic weighting is the more widely used choice for ordinal scoring because it is much more lenient about near-misses and steeply intolerant of large errors — which matches human intuition about ordinal mistakes — and because it has the convenient property of equaling a chance-corrected version of the squared-error correlation between rater scores [3]. Quadratic-weighted Cohen's kappa (QWK) became the de facto metric for high-profile ordinal-prediction competitions, including the Hewlett Foundation's Automated Student Assessment Prize and the Kaggle Diabetic Retinopathy challenge, and it is now standard in the ordinal-classification deep-learning literature [4]. The choice between linear and quadratic weighting is not cosmetic: the same prediction-vs-truth confusion table can yield κ_linear ≈ 0.55 and κ_quadratic ≈ 0.78 because the quadratic scheme rewards a judge whose errors are mostly off-by-one. For job-fit scoring on a small ordinal scale (e.g. 1–5), QWK is a defensible primary metric for ordinal-aware agreement, but it does not by itself diagnose whether the *distribution* of judge scores matches the distribution of true scores.

Krippendorff's alpha [5] generalizes the agreement-coefficient family further. The 2011 Annenberg School technical report — Krippendorff's most-cited methodological paper — defines α in terms of observed and expected disagreement and admits arbitrary metric difference functions on the categories: nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio, polar, and circular. For ordinal data, the ordinal difference function is δ²(v, v′) = (Σ_{g=v}^{v′} n_g − (n_v + n_v′)/2)², which weights disagreements not just by ordinal distance but by the rank density between the two categories — a category jump that crosses many other ratings is penalized more than a jump across a sparsely populated region. This is conceptually a stricter and more rank-aware penalty than QWK's fixed (i − j)² weighting. Three properties make α particularly attractive over ICC and weighted κ in LLM-judge settings. First, α handles missing data natively, which matters when an enrichment cascade fails on some jobs and the judge produces no score for them. Second, α generalizes to any number of raters without redefinition, so a panel of three LLM judges plus a human gold rater can be summarized by a single coefficient. Third, α is grounded in a unified disagreement-as-distance framework that makes it more directly comparable across nominal (e.g. apply/skip) and ordinal (1–5) outcomes. Krippendorff [5] explicitly recommends α ≥ 0.80 for high-stakes inferences and α ≥ 0.667 as a tentative-conclusion threshold; these are the thresholds that have since been adopted in NLP annotation work [12].

The distinction between *agreement* and *reliability* has been a persistent source of confusion in the applied literature, and Gisev, Bell, and Chen [6] make this explicit in their 2013 review for *Research in Social and Administrative Pharmacy*. Inter-rater agreement (IRA) measures the extent to which raters give *concordant* responses — i.e. their absolute values match — while inter-rater reliability (IRR) measures the extent to which raters *consistently distinguish* between different items. The classic illustrative example: a rater who is always exactly one point lower than the gold rater has IRA = 0 (never identical) but IRR = 1 (perfectly consistent). For an ordinal job-fit scorer this distinction is the entire ball game. A judge that ranks jobs perfectly but inflates every score by +1.5 has perfect IRR (Pearson r = 1.0, ICC(3,1) ≈ 1.0) and disastrous IRA (ICC(2,1) ≈ 0.4, signed mean error = +1.5). Critically, *calibration*, in the sense used in this design document, is closer to IRA than IRR: calibration asks whether the judge's score *matches* the true score on the same scale, not whether it preserves the ranking. Reporting Pearson *r* alone — which captures only monotonic association — therefore systematically understates calibration error in exactly the failure mode that matters for any decision rule that thresholds on absolute score (e.g. "apply if score ≥ 3"). The applied-statistics literature has long noted that Pearson *r* on ordinal data also tends to *underestimate* the strength of monotone but non-linear relationships, so in either direction it is the wrong primary metric for an ordinal-scoring audit [7].

Three recent (2023–2026) LLM-eval papers establish that these classical concerns become acute, not academic, when the rater is an LLM. Zheng et al. [8], in the foundational *Judging LLM-as-a-Judge* paper accompanying MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena, demonstrate that GPT-4-class judges achieve over 80% pairwise agreement with human preferences — comparable to inter-human agreement — but they evaluate primarily on pairwise A/B comparisons, not on absolute ordinal scores. Their main contribution to the present discussion is the cataloguing of judge biases (position, verbosity, self-enhancement) that systematically distort scores rather than introducing pure noise; bias of this form is invisible to Pearson *r* and ICC(3,1) but visible to ICC(2,1), QWK, and signed-error metrics. Liu et al. [9] (G-Eval) propose probability-weighted scoring — taking the LLM's per-score token logprobs and computing an expected score over the rating tokens — explicitly because they observe that direct integer scoring produces an extremely tied score distribution. G-Eval's continuous-expected-score variant achieves Spearman ρ = 0.514 with humans on summarization, substantially above the integer-output baseline; the implication is that the discrete nature of the integer output is itself a source of measurement degradation, even before agreement is computed.

The score-distribution-collapse phenomenon is established directly by Wang et al. [10] in *TrustJudge* (ICLR 2026). They demonstrate explicitly that "score-comparison inconsistency primarily stems from information loss in the integer scoring system — the coarse-grained 5-point scale compresses nuanced quality differences into identical scores," and they show that 23% of ordinal scoring decisions are inconsistent with the same model's pairwise comparisons. Their proposed remedy — distribution-sensitive scoring that computes continuous expectations from the discrete rating-token probabilities — reduces inconsistency by 8.4 absolute percentage points. Although TrustJudge frames the problem on a 5-point scale, the principle generalizes: even when an LLM is prompted on a 0–100 scale, recent work [11] shows the model concentrates probability mass on a small set of preferred multiples (typically values like 0, 25, 50, 75, 80, 85, 90, 95, 100), so the *effective* resolution of a "0–100 scale" is closer to 5–10 distinct values in practice. Li et al. [11] in their 2026 "Grading Scale Impact" study use ICC(2,1) (the Shrout-Fleiss absolute-agreement form) to compare 0–5, 0–10, and 0–100 scales across six benchmarks and find that human-LLM alignment is *highest* on 0–5 and *lowest* on 0–10, with 0–100 in between but inferior to 0–5; they directly attribute the 0–10 and 0–100 deficits to inconsistent rounding and preferred-value clustering. Haldar and Hockenmaier [12] in *Rating Roulette* (EMNLP 2025) extend the picture to *intra-rater* reliability: across multiple runs with different random seeds, the same LLM produces inconsistent scores for the same input, with intra-rater Krippendorff's α frequently below 0.5 — a level the literature [5] regards as unfit for any inferential use. They explicitly recommend Krippendorff's α (rather than Pearson *r*) for diagnosing this failure mode because α is sensitive to absolute disagreement and tolerates the missing-data structure of multi-run experiments.

Synthesizing across these sources, the methodological consensus for evaluating an ordinal LLM judge is: (a) Pearson *r* alone is systematically misleading because it is invariant to additive and multiplicative biases that are exactly the failure modes LLM judges exhibit [6], [8]; (b) the agreement metric should be ordinal-aware — quadratic-weighted Cohen's κ [3], [4] or Krippendorff's α with the ordinal difference function [5] — and computed alongside ICC(2,1) for absolute agreement [1], [2]; (c) signed mean error and mean absolute error must be reported because they are the only metrics that surface the systematic bias direction [6]; (d) the eval should separately report calibration (does the judge's score distribution match ground-truth?) from agreement (do individual scores match?) because the LLM-judge literature documents that scores collapse to preferred values even when ranking is preserved [10], [11]; and (e) intra-rater reliability across reseeded runs is itself a metric to track, because the LLM rater is non-deterministic and may produce different scores on the same input [12]. For the recalibration milestone informing this survey, the practical implication is that the eval harness should compute, at minimum: ICC(2,1), quadratic-weighted κ, Krippendorff's ordinal α, mean absolute error, signed mean error, and a histogram of unique values produced — with Pearson *r* retained only as a secondary monotonicity check, not as a primary calibration metric.
