# Topic 1: LLM-as-Judge — Core Findings and Bias Taxonomy

## Foundations of the LLM-as-Judge Paradigm

The LLM-as-judge paradigm was established as a serious alternative to human evaluation by Zheng et al. with their MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena work [1]. Confronting the inadequacy of static benchmarks for measuring open-ended chat-assistant quality, the authors introduced MT-Bench (a curated multi-turn question set) and Chatbot Arena (a pairwise human-preference platform), then asked whether strong LLMs could substitute for the expensive human judges those benchmarks rely on. Across roughly 3,000 expert votes and 30,000 crowdsourced conversations, GPT-4 achieved over 80% agreement with human pairwise preferences — comparable to inter-human agreement on the same items. That headline result legitimized LLM judges as a scalable evaluation tool, but the same paper also enumerated four systematic failure modes: position bias, verbosity bias, self-enhancement bias, and limited reasoning ability. The order in which Zheng et al. listed those biases is itself a useful map of the field's first-order concerns, and most subsequent work either deepens one of those four categories or proposes a structured mitigation for it.

In parallel, Liu et al.'s G-Eval [2] approached evaluation from a different angle — single-output scoring of natural-language-generation tasks rather than pairwise chat comparison. G-Eval combines chain-of-thought reasoning with a form-filling protocol in which the LLM produces sub-scores against rubric dimensions, and reports a Spearman correlation of 0.514 with human ratings on summarization, the strongest result on that benchmark family at publication. Critically, Liu et al. were among the first to surface the *self-bias* phenomenon at scale: GPT-4-as-judge systematically rated GPT-generated summaries higher than equivalently-rated human-written summaries, a finding the authors flagged as a potential issue rather than fully diagnosing. G-Eval's rubric-decomposition protocol also foreshadowed a later debate over pointwise versus pairwise scoring discussed below. Both [1] and [2] established a common methodological pattern — quantify agreement with human raters, then catalog disagreement modes — that the next generation of bias-focused papers refined.

The PandaLM project by Wang et al. [3] complemented these closed-source efforts by training an open-source 7B-parameter judge fine-tuned from LLaMA, achieving 93.75% of GPT-3.5's evaluation quality and 88.28% of GPT-4's measured by F1 on a human-annotated test set. PandaLM's contribution to the bias literature is methodological: by treating judgment as a discriminative classification task with subjective criteria (conciseness, clarity, instruction-following, comprehensiveness, formality), it produced training data that downstream bias studies could leverage as a non-API baseline.

## The Bias Taxonomy: Position, Verbosity, Self-Preference

The most consequential follow-up to Zheng et al.'s position-bias claim is Wang et al.'s "Large Language Models are not Fair Evaluators" [4]. They demonstrated that swapping the order of two candidate responses can completely flip a pairwise judgment in striking cases — Vicuna-13B was rated superior to ChatGPT on 66 of 80 queries when placed first, but the result reversed when placed second. This is a far more severe operationalization of position bias than [1]'s framing, because it shows the bias is not merely a slight tilt but can dominate the actual quality signal. Wang et al. proposed three mitigations: Multiple Evidence Calibration (require the judge to articulate evidence before scoring), Balanced Position Calibration (run both orderings and aggregate), and Human-in-the-Loop Calibration triggered by a balanced-position diversity entropy threshold. Balanced Position Calibration in particular has become a near-default convention in the field, though it doubles inference cost.

Shi et al. extended this analysis with a systematic study across 15 LLM judges and over 150,000 evaluation instances [5]. Their key contribution is a tripartite metric framework — repetition stability, position consistency, and preference fairness — that lets practitioners decompose what kind of position bias a particular judge exhibits. They find that position bias is "not due to random chance and varies significantly across judges and tasks," that its strength is only weakly correlated with prompt length, and (most usefully for rubric design) that it is *strongly* affected by the quality gap between solutions: when two candidates are close in quality, position effects dominate; when one is clearly better, position bias collapses. This finding has direct implications for ordinal-scoring rubrics that operate near decision boundaries — exactly the regime in which a job-fit pipeline's "borderline 3" scores live.

The self-preference branch was put on rigorous causal footing by Panickssery et al. [6]. They show that GPT-4 and Llama-2 can distinguish their own generations from other models' outputs with non-trivial accuracy, and — by fine-tuning models to vary their self-recognition capability — establish a *linear* causal relationship between self-recognition strength and the magnitude of self-preference inflation. Earlier work [1, 2] had observed self-enhancement; [6] is the paper that demonstrates the mechanism is not a mere correlation but a recognition-driven preference. Wataoka et al. [7] subsequently refined this hypothesis: the apparent self-preference is better explained by *low perplexity* on familiar text. LLMs assign higher scores to lower-perplexity outputs regardless of who generated them, so what looks like narcissism is partly a fluency-calibration problem. This refinement matters for rubric design — anchoring scores to perplexity-stable rubric language (rather than free-form judge prose) should reduce the bias.

## Scoring-Specific Biases: Beyond Pairwise

A weakness of [1] [4] [5] is that their bias evidence is overwhelmingly pairwise; many production pipelines (including ordinal job-fit scorers) use *pointwise absolute scoring* on a 1–5 or 1–10 scale, where different failure modes apply. Li et al. [8] performed the first dedicated audit of scoring bias in this regime and identified three previously undocumented bias types: rubric order bias (the order in which rubric criteria are presented affects the score), score ID bias (the symbolic identifiers on the scale — "1 to 5" versus "A to E" versus "low/medium/high" — affect the result independently of semantic content), and reference-answer score bias (the score assigned to a provided reference example anchors the judgment of subsequent items). Their experiments on advanced LLMs show all three biases are substantial, though the paper does not publish standardized effect sizes in its abstract. For a recalibration effort that swaps rubric structure, [8] is essential reading: its findings imply that even minor rubric revisions can shift the score distribution without any change in underlying judge competence.

Tripathi et al. [9] complement [8] by directly comparing pairwise versus pointwise feedback protocols under adversarial perturbation. They report that pairwise preferences flip in approximately 35% of cases when distractor features are injected, while absolute (pointwise) scores flip only in 9% of cases. This is a striking inversion of conventional wisdom, which often holds pairwise as more reliable. Tripathi et al. argue absolute scoring is "more robust to manipulation, producing judgments that better reflect response quality." For an ordinal job-scoring rubric this is reassuring — but it sharpens the importance of [8]'s scoring-specific biases, since absolute scoring's robustness only holds if the rubric itself is well-designed.

## Central Tendency and Aggregation

The central-tendency-bias problem — judges clustering scores around the middle of an ordinal scale — has received less direct empirical study than position or verbosity bias, but Wang, Zhang, and Choi [10] supply the most useful methodological intervention for it. Rather than taking the *mode* of a judge's output (greedy decoding's single token), they propose taking the *mean* of the full judgment-token probability distribution, treating the score as expected value over the distribution. Their EMNLP 2025 Findings paper shows this consistently outperforms greedy decoding across evaluation formats, and that incorporating *risk aversion* into the aggregation further improves performance. They also caution that chain-of-thought prompting can compress the distribution and *hurt* performance under their protocol — a direct counterpoint to G-Eval's CoT-driven design [2]. The implication for rubric design is concrete: if the judge's output token distribution is read directly (not just the argmax), central-tendency clustering at the score level can be partially undone post-hoc, because the distribution typically retains discriminative shape even when the mode is "3."

## Cross-Cutting Surveys and Cognitive Biases

Chen et al. [11] expanded the bias frame beyond purely structural concerns (position, length) to *cognitive* biases borrowed from the psychometric literature: Misinformation Oversight, Gender, Authority, and Beauty biases. They show that both human and LLM judges are vulnerable to perturbations that exploit these biases, and that LLM judges can in fact be *attacked* by crafting inputs that invoke them. This finding undercuts the comfortable narrative from [1] that LLM judges merely mirror human judgment quality — they replicate human cognitive failure modes too, and may be more exploitable in adversarial settings. For an evaluation pipeline that must be robust over time, [11] argues for explicit perturbation testing as part of any rubric-design loop.

Finally, Chan et al.'s ChatEval [12] proposes multi-agent debate as a structural mitigation for the entire bias family rather than addressing biases individually. Their referee-team architecture, in which multiple LLM agents argue across rounds before issuing a verdict, improved evaluation accuracy by 6.2% over single-agent ChatGPT and 2.5% over single-agent GPT-4 on their benchmark. The smaller GPT-4 gain is itself informative: stronger single judges leave less room for ensemble methods, suggesting that for a sufficiently capable scorer, simpler bias-mitigation strategies (balanced position calibration, mean-of-distribution aggregation) may be more cost-effective than multi-agent ensembles.

## Synthesis and Open Questions

Across these papers, several patterns stand out for a recalibration effort. First, *mitigations have measurable but bounded effect sizes*: balanced-position calibration roughly halves the swap-flip rate [4] but does not eliminate it; multi-agent debate yields single-digit accuracy gains [12]; mean-aggregation of judgment distributions improves over greedy decoding consistently but modestly [10]. There is no silver-bullet intervention. Second, *pointwise scoring is structurally distinct from pairwise* and inherits a different bias profile — robust to adversarial pairwise tricks [9] but vulnerable to rubric-presentation effects [8]. Third, the literature on *central tendency bias as a distribution-shape problem* is comparatively thin, especially for ordinal scales of size five or seven; [10] is the most directly relevant intervention but operates on token distributions, not on score outputs. This is a clear gap that the present project may need to address with its own measurement, rather than relying on prior effect-size estimates.
