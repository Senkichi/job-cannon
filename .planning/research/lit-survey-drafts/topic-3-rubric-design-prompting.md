# Rubric Design and Scoring Prompt Techniques for LLM-as-Judge

## Introduction

When an LLM is asked to assign an ordinal score on a Likert-style scale, the prompt itself is the measurement instrument. Two prompts that elicit the "same" 1–5 score can produce wildly different distributions, inter-rater agreements, and human correlations depending on what anchors are written, whether a reference answer is supplied, when chain-of-thought is emitted, how few-shot examples are ordered, and whether the output is free-form or schema-constrained. This section surveys the rubric and prompt-engineering literature that bears directly on those design choices.

## Reference-Free Scoring with Chain-of-Thought: The G-Eval Template

The most influential reference-free scoring template is G-Eval [1], which couples a written rubric with a model-generated "chain of evaluation steps" and a form-filling output. Liu et al. show that on SummEval and Topical-Chat, GPT-4 prompted in this way achieves a Spearman correlation of 0.514 with human judges on summarization, substantially above ROUGE and BERTScore baselines. Two design decisions inside G-Eval matter for our purposes. First, the auto-CoT step is generated *once*, ahead of time, and then frozen into the scoring prompt — the rationale is produced before the score, never after. Second, the actual numeric output is constrained to a small integer set and the authors recommend taking a probability-weighted expectation over the score tokens to recover continuous resolution. Liu et al. also flag a worrying limitation: the auto-CoT does not always improve correlation, and on some dimensions it slightly hurts. This is the first piece of empirical evidence that "more reasoning" is not monotonically beneficial — a finding that recurs throughout the literature.

## Fine-Grained, Per-Criterion Rubrics: FLASK, Prometheus, and BiGGen-Bench

Where G-Eval scores a single dimension at a time, a parallel line of work argues that decomposing the rubric into fine-grained skill-specific criteria is itself a calibration intervention. FLASK [2] decomposes evaluation into 12 fine-grained skills (e.g., Logical Correctness, Factuality, Conciseness) grouped under four primary abilities, and assigns a separate 1–5 score per skill with skill-specific anchors. Ye et al. find that this decomposition increases human-LLM agreement and reduces evaluator bias relative to a single coarse "helpfulness" score, and that the gain comes specifically from forcing the evaluator to reason about distinct axes rather than collapsing them into a holistic judgment. The methodological caveat is that FLASK was validated on instruction-following corpora, where skills are reasonably orthogonal; for tasks where criteria are highly correlated (as in candidate-job fit, where "skill match" and "experience level" are entangled), the per-skill gain may be smaller.

Prometheus [3] takes the rubric-decomposition idea further by training a 13B open-source evaluator specifically on user-supplied rubrics. Kim et al. construct the Feedback Collection — 1K fine-grained score rubrics, 20K instructions, 100K GPT-4-generated responses with feedback — and use it to fine-tune Llama-2 13B into Prometheus. The crucial design choice here is that every rubric in the training set is *fully anchored*: each of the five score levels has a written description specific to that instruction's evaluation criterion, not generic "1=poor, 5=excellent" anchors. Prometheus achieves Pearson r = 0.897 with humans across 45 customized rubrics, on par with GPT-4 (r = 0.882) and far above ChatGPT (r = 0.392). The paper also requires a reference answer alongside the rubric, and reports that removing it materially degrades agreement — a finding that directly informs the reference-based vs. reference-free debate.

BiGGen-Bench [4] generalizes the Prometheus approach to 77 tasks and nine capabilities, again with *instance-specific* rubrics anchored at every point of a 5-point Likert scale. Kim et al. evaluate 103 frontier LMs with five evaluator LMs and report that instance-level rubrics close most of the gap between human and LM evaluators that abstract criteria like "helpfulness" leave open. The combined evidence from FLASK, Prometheus, and BiGGen-Bench is that *anchoring every point of the scale* — not just the endpoints — is consistently associated with higher human alignment, and that the labor cost of writing per-instruction rubrics is justified by the calibration gain.

## When the Rationale Should Appear

A practical question for any scoring prompt is whether the model should emit its reasoning before, after, or instead of the score. JudgeLM [5] examines this in the fine-tuning regime and identifies three judge-specific biases — position bias, knowledge bias, and format bias — that emerge when models are trained to score with rigid templates. Zhu et al. introduce three mitigations (swap augmentation, reference support, and reference drop), and crucially their training format places the critique before the numeric judgment. They report that this ordering substantially improves agreement with the GPT-4 teacher, exceeding 90% inter-annotator agreement.

Auto-J [6] reaches a similar conclusion from a different angle. Li et al. train a 13B generative judge that emits a structured natural-language critique first and then a final scalar score or pairwise verdict, arguing that the critique-first format is what makes the model's judgments interpretable and post-hoc auditable. While Auto-J does not run the ablation that swaps the order, the consistent design across Prometheus, JudgeLM, and Auto-J — all of which emit reasoning before the score — reflects an emerging consensus that score-first prompting risks the rationale becoming a *post-hoc rationalization* of an already-committed numeric anchor rather than a faithful basis for it. G-Eval's auto-CoT design [1] is the same pattern at the prompt level: reasoning first, score last.

## Few-Shot Calibration and Demonstration Order

Few-shot examples in scoring prompts function differently than in classification: they teach the model what a "3" or a "4" looks like rather than which class label to emit. Zhao et al.'s influential "Calibrate Before Use" study [7] documents three biases that arise specifically from few-shot demonstrations — *majority label bias* (preferring the most common label in shots), *recency bias* (preferring the label that appears closest to the query), and *common token bias* (preferring tokens frequent in pretraining). The position effect is large enough that simply reordering identical demonstrations can change accuracy by 30+ absolute percentage points on classification tasks. For ordinal scoring, the implication is that few-shot exemplars should be ordered carefully — Zhao et al. recommend either contextual calibration (estimating bias with a content-free probe and subtracting it) or randomization across calibration runs.

The diversity of demonstrations is a second axis. Yang et al. [8] systematically vary demonstration similarity vs. diversity across Llama-3.1, Gemma-2, and Mistral-v0.3 and find that diversity-aware selection improves performance on complex tasks (math, code) and increases robustness to out-of-distribution queries, while similarity-only selection works for simple sentiment-style tasks. For scoring rubrics in domains with edge cases (e.g., a candidate whose experience pattern is unusual), this argues for explicitly including hard-case exemplars rather than only "typical" ones. Diminishing returns past roughly 4–8 shots is a recurring empirical pattern across both [7] and [8], though the exact knee depends on context length and model scale.

## Structured Output: A Calibration Trade-Off

A common production temptation is to wrap the score in a JSON schema and use constrained decoding. Tam et al. [9] run the most direct test of this assumption to date, comparing free-text, JSON-mode, and constrained-decoding outputs across reasoning, classification, and extraction tasks. They report that strict format constraints cause a measurable decline in reasoning ability — 10–30% on hard reasoning benchmarks — while *helping* on simple classification. The mechanism, they argue, is that the model spends representational capacity on adhering to syntax rather than reasoning. For LLM-as-judge, this trade-off is especially relevant when the rubric demands genuine multi-criterion reasoning: a free-form rationale block followed by a single short JSON envelope at the end may calibrate better than fully nested JSON-from-the-start.

Lee et al. [10] independently study the consistency of LLM evaluators across scales and rubric granularities, and find that strong proprietary models are not necessarily consistent — self-consistency (re-running the same input) and inter-scale consistency (1–5 vs. 1–10) are dimensions on which top models can fail even when their human correlation is high. This bears on rubric design: collapsing to a smaller scale (e.g., 1–4 with no neutral midpoint) and writing dense per-point anchors tends to improve consistency, because the model has less latitude to drift between near-equivalent labels.

## Synthesis for Rubric Design

The literature converges on several actionable choices for an ordinal scoring rubric. First, fully anchor every point of the scale with criterion-specific descriptions ([2], [3], [4]) rather than only endpoints — full anchoring narrows distributional drift and increases human alignment. Second, place the rationale before the score ([1], [5], [6]); score-first prompting risks rationalization rather than reasoning. Third, prefer per-criterion decomposition with separate scores when criteria are reasonably independent [2], reverting to a single overall score otherwise. Fourth, when using few-shot exemplars, include diverse and hard cases [8] and either randomize or calibrate against demonstration order [7]. Fifth, treat structured output as a calibration trade-off [9]: a free-form rationale followed by a small structured envelope tends to outperform fully constrained JSON for reasoning-heavy rubrics. Sixth, evaluate the rubric on consistency, not just correlation [10] — two prompts with identical human-correlation scores can have very different self-consistency properties, and the more consistent one is the safer production choice.
